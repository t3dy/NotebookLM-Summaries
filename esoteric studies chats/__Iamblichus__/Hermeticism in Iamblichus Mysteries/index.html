The hermetic material in *Iamblichus: On the Mysteries* is interwoven with the defense of theurgy and the integration of Neoplatonism with Egyptian religious and mystical traditions. Here’s a summary of the main Hermetic elements: ### 1. Hermetic Context in the Theurgy Discussion - Iamblichus situates theurgy as a divine practice aligned with the Hermetic worldview, emphasizing its ability to elevate the soul beyond the material realm. Theurgy is presented as a means of achieving union with the divine, echoing the Hermetic goal of spiritual ascent. ### 2. Egyptian Religious Practices - Drawing from Egyptian traditions, which heavily influenced the Hermetic texts, Iamblichus describes rituals and symbols as mediating connections between humans and the gods. His accounts often resonate with the *Corpus Hermeticum*, particularly in the portrayal of intermediaries such as daemons and divine beings. ### 3. Role of Divine Names and Symbols - Echoing Hermetic texts, Iamblichus underscores the power of divine names and sacred symbols. These are not mere human constructs but are believed to carry intrinsic divine potency, crucial for effective theurgical rites. ### 4. The Concept of Divine Hierarchies - Hermetic cosmology, with its layers of celestial beings and gods, finds a parallel in Iamblichus’s detailed hierarchies of divine entities. These hierarchies serve as a framework for theurgic practices, bridging human and divine realms. ### 5. Critique of Rationalism - Iamblichus’s critique of Porphyry’s rationalist stance aligns with Hermetic texts' preference for mystical and intuitive knowledge over purely intellectual pursuits. He defends the value of rituals and divine invocations as surpassing human reason, a core Hermetic theme. ### 6. Pseudonym of Abamon - Writing as "Abamon," Iamblichus connects himself to Egyptian wisdom traditions, much like the Hermetic corpus often attributes its teachings to the mythical Hermes Trismegistus. This pseudonym serves to authenticate his arguments with the authority of ancient Egyptian sages. No, the word theurgy (*theourgia*, meaning "god-work" or "divine action") does not explicitly appear in the *Corpus Hermeticum*. The Hermetic texts focus on similar concepts, such as divine ascent, spiritual practices, and the transformation of the soul, but they do not use the specific term *theurgy*, which is more commonly associated with Neoplatonism, particularly with Iamblichus and later Platonic philosophers. In the *Corpus Hermeticum*, terms and ideas related to theurgy are present in spirit, focusing on: 1. Divine Ascent and Union: Practices and meditations described in texts like *CH I* and *CH XIII* aim for the soul's union with the divine. 2. Sacred Knowledge (Gnosis): The emphasis on acquiring knowledge through divine illumination parallels the goals of theurgy. 3. Invocation of the Divine: References to prayers, invocations, and the use of sacred names resonate with theurgical rituals, even if they are not explicitly called that. The concept of theurgy, as articulated by Iamblichus in *On the Mysteries*, formalizes these ideas into a structured philosophy and practice that bridges ritual action and philosophical insight. The *Corpus Hermeticum* serves as a foundational influence but does not directly articulate theurgical practices as a formal discipline.
