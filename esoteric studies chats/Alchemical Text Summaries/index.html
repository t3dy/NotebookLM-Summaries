The True Oil of Sulphur per Campanam by George Starkey (pp. 37-54): In this treatise, George Starkey, an influential alchemist and disciple of Paracelsian medicine, details the preparation and uses of a potent medicinal substance called the *Oil of Sulphur*, distilled directly from burning sulfur. Historical Context and Purpose (pp. 37-41): Starkey introduces his account by referencing the eminent alchemist Helmont, who recounts a case from 1600 where he prescribed the oil of sulfur to a middle-aged man worried about his family’s future welfare. The man’s desire for longevity led him to seek a remedy that would preserve his health and extend his life. Starkey’s narrative follows Helmont’s prescription of the oil, taken in small, carefully controlled doses, which purportedly kept the man healthy and active for over forty years, even aiding in the healing of a broken leg in his old age. 2. Preparation and Efficacy of the Oil (pp. 41-45): Starkey explains that the oil is prepared by setting sulfur on fire and condensing its fumes into a liquid through a process using a special glass apparatus called a “campana,” or bell-shaped vessel. This oil is unique because it is pure and unadulterated, containing the vital sulfuric essence, which Starkey claims preserves and revitalizes the human body. In his observations, this oil has the potential to prevent common ailments and delay the onset of age-related decline by purifying the blood, which Starkey poetically describes as “the very wine of our life.” 3. Medicinal Applications (pp. 43-46): Starkey emphasizes the oil’s broad range of applications. It serves as a preservative for both organic matter and consumables, such as beer, wine, and meat. Additionally, it can be used to treat dental ailments; when diluted, it cleans and whitens teeth and protects them from decay. The oil also alleviates conditions like hoarseness, cough, and other respiratory complaints by gargling, or even through its vaporous fumes. Furthermore, Starkey mentions its use in preventing rheums (mucous discharges) and easing headaches and sore throats, demonstrating its multipurpose value as both an external and internal remedy. 4. The Oil’s Unique Properties and Potency (pp. 46-49): Starkey underscores the oil’s potent preservative properties, describing it as both an acidic and balsamic remedy that combats putrefaction and enhances the body’s resistance to fevers, infections, and even plague-like illnesses. Starkey criticizes the common practice of adulterating sulfur oil with cheaper vitriolic acid (from sulfuric acid), explaining that genuine oil of sulfur should be clear, sharp, and able to preserve both organic and inorganic matter. Warnings Against Counterfeit Products (pp. 47-51): Starkey vehemently warns against the “adulterated mineral acidity of vitriol,” which he claims many apothecaries and chemists sell under the guise of sulfur oil. This imitation oil, created by distilling sulfur mixed with vitriol, is inferior in its medicinal properties and can even be harmful, lacking the true efficacy and preservative qualities of authentic sulfur oil. Starkey insists that the true oil is identifiable by its ability to restore wine, prevent putrefaction, and resist alteration upon contact with mercury—a quality the counterfeit oil cannot mimic. He recommends that only those trained in alchemy produce this oil, asserting that authenticity can be ensured by sourcing it from specific, reputable chemists in London. 6. Philosophical and Alchemical Importance (pp. 49-52): Starkey extends his discussion to the broader alchemical principles behind the oil’s properties, describing how it represents a perfected sulfuric essence that combines both the mercurial and sulfuric principles of alchemy. The oil’s properties align it with the alchemical ideals of purification, transformation, and preservation, marking it as a significant medicinal and alchemical achievement. 7. Starkey also addresses the tragic passing of Mr. Starkey, who reportedly died of the plague in 1665 after examining an infected corpse. Despite this loss, Starkey assures that the knowledge of sulfur oil remains accessible, and those inclined can attempt its production themselves, provided they have access to unprocessed mineral sulfur and the right apparatus. The *True Oil of Sulfur* embodies Starkey’s commitment to genuine alchemical practice and his disdain for the fraudulent substitutions that plague the field.
