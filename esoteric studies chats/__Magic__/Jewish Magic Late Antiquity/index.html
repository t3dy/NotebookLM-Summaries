For instance, Wasserstrom points to several texts that detail the creation of amulets intended to protect individuals from disease or misfortune, demonstrating the everyday reliance on magical solutions in the absence of other medical or protective means. #### The Intersection of Magic and Religion Wasserstrom argues that magic and religion were not seen as mutually exclusive by medieval Jews. Rather, magical practices were often intertwined with religious rituals and beliefs. This is evidenced by the fact that many magical texts from the Geniza include references to God, angels, and biblical verses, suggesting that magic was viewed as a way of accessing divine power for practical purposes. The use of sacred names in spells, such as the Tetragrammaton (YHWH), further illustrates the connection between magic and religion (pg. 275). The chapter delves into the role of religious authorities in regulating or endorsing magical practices. While some rabbinical authorities condemned certain types of magic, others seemed to accept or even promote magical practices that aligned with religious principles. This ambivalence toward magic reflects the complexity of its role within Jewish religious life during the Geniza period. #### The Cairo Geniza as a Source of Magical Knowledge The Cairo Geniza, a treasure trove of medieval Jewish manuscripts, offers invaluable insights into the magical practices of Jewish communities. Wasserstrom emphasizes that many of the magical texts found in the Geniza are unique, providing details about specific spells, rituals, and incantations that were used by Jews in the Islamic world. He highlights the diversity of magical traditions preserved in the Geniza, which include influences from Babylonian, Greco-Roman, and Islamic magical traditions (pg. 279). One particularly interesting aspect of the Geniza’s magical texts is the use of the Hebrew language alongside Aramaic and Arabic, reflecting the multilingual nature of the Jewish communities in this period. These texts also include magical recipes and instructions for creating amulets, invoking angels, and performing divination. The inclusion of magical texts in the Geniza documents suggests that magic was not only practiced by isolated individuals but was part of a broader communal tradition. #### The Social Function of Magic Wasserstrom explores the social dimensions of magic, suggesting that it served as a tool for negotiating power and social relationships within the Jewish community. For example, magic was often used to influence legal decisions or to gain favor with powerful individuals. Some texts detail spells intended to sway the outcome of court cases or to gain an advantage in business transactions. This indicates that magic was not merely a form of personal spiritual practice but was also a means of navigating the social and economic structures of the time (pg. 283). The chapter also touches upon the role of women in magical practices, noting that many of the magical texts from the Geniza are addressed to or created by women. This suggests that women played an active role in the production and use of magic, particularly in the domestic sphere. Women’s involvement in magic highlights the gendered nature of certain magical practices, such as fertility spells and protective amulets for children. #### Conclusion: Magic as a Reflection of Social and Religious Life In his conclusion, Wasserstrom argues that magic provides a unique window into the social and religious life of medieval Jewish communities. The magical texts of the Geniza reflect the concerns, beliefs, and practices of ordinary Jews, showing that magic was an integral part of their daily lives. Rather than being seen as a fringe or illicit activity, magic was woven into the fabric of Jewish society, functioning alongside religious rituals and social structures. Wasserstrom concludes by calling for further study of the Geniza’s magical texts, suggesting that they offer valuable insights into the intersection of religion, society, and magic in the medieval Jewish world.
