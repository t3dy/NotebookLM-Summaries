Key Passages: - "Oneirocritica," Book I, Preface Quotation: > "I have collected my material from the best sources, from experience, and from a long study of the works of the ancients." — Artemidorus, *The Interpretation of Dreams*, trans. Robert J. White (Noyes Press, 1975). - Symbol Interpretation (e.g., Book II, Chapters 1–10) Explanation: Artemidorus provides detailed interpretations of specific dream symbols, categorizing them according to their significance. --- #### 5. Plato's "Republic" Context: Plato discusses dreams in relation to the soul and moral psychology. Key Passage: - "Republic," Book IX, 571c–572b Quotation: > "In sleep, when the better part of the soul is inactive, the beastly and savage part, filled with food and drink, rises up and shakes off sleep to seek what satisfies its own instincts." — *Plato, Republic*, trans. G. M. A. Grube, rev. C. D. C. Reeve (Hackett Publishing, 1992). Explanation: Plato considers dreams as revealing the irrational desires of the soul, suggesting that without rational control, base instincts emerge during sleep. --- #### 6. Plotinus's "Enneads" Context: As a leading Neoplatonic philosopher, Plotinus explores the nature of reality and the soul's experiences. Key Passage: - "Enneads," IV.8.1 ("On the Descent of the Soul into Bodies") Quotation: > "In sleep, the soul withdraws into itself, and in that state, it may gain access to the intelligible realm and behold truths it cannot see when bound to the senses." — *Plotinus, The Enneads*, trans. Stephen MacKenna (Larson Publications, 1992). Explanation: Plotinus views dreams as opportunities for the soul to connect with higher realities beyond the physical world. --- ### Comparison with Talmudic and Kabbalistic Views --- #### Similarities 1. Dreams as a Means of Divine or Spiritual Communication - Greek Sources: Homer's classification of dreams through gates of horn (truth) and ivory (deception) implies a belief in true dreams conveying messages from the divine. - Talmudic Sources: The Talmud states, "A dream is one-sixtieth of prophecy" (*Babylonian Talmud, Berakhot 57b*), acknowledging the potential prophetic nature of dreams. - Kabbalistic Views: The Zohar suggests that during sleep, the soul ascends and can receive spiritual insights (*Zohar I, 183a*). 2. Importance of Interpretation - Greek Sources: Artemidorus emphasizes skilled interpretation, noting that the meaning of a dream depends on individual circumstances. - Talmudic Sources: "All dreams follow the mouth" (*Berakhot 55b*), meaning that the interpretation given can influence the outcome. 3. Systematic Symbolism - Both traditions compile lists of symbols and their meanings. - Artemidorus's Oneirocritica: Detailed analysis of dream symbols. - Talmudic Examples: Specific interpretations, such as seeing a well indicating peace (*Berakhot 56b*). #### Differences 1. Philosophical vs. Religious Perspectives - Greek Philosophers (Aristotle): Tended to naturalize dreams, viewing them as psychological phenomena without inherent divine significance. - Talmudic/Kabbalistic Sources: Often embrace dreams as potential channels for divine messages, with a stronger emphasis on their spiritual significance. 2. Moral and Ethical Interpretations - Plato: Dreams can reveal the irrational and base desires of individuals. - Talmud/Kabbalah: Dreams can serve as prompts for moral and spiritual self-improvement. 3. Approach to Prophecy - Greek Tradition: Prophetic dreams are acknowledged but often treated with skepticism or as rare phenomena. - Jewish Tradition: Dreams are a common means through which God communicates with individuals, albeit requiring careful interpretation. --- ### Originality vs. Dependence --- #### Originality - Independent Development: Both Greek and Jewish traditions developed their dream interpretation practices within their unique cultural and religious contexts. - Distinct Philosophical Foundations: - Greek Thought: Rooted in polytheism and philosophical inquiry, exploring dreams through mythology, philosophy, and early scientific thought. - Jewish Thought: Grounded in monotheism and a covenantal relationship with God, viewing dreams as part of divine communication and spiritual practice. #### Potential Influences - Historical Interaction: - During the Hellenistic period, there was significant cultural exchange between Greek and Jewish societies, particularly in Alexandria. - Some scholars suggest that Jewish thinkers may have been influenced by Greek philosophy, including ideas about dreams. - Scholarly Perspectives: - Martin Hengel argues that Hellenistic culture influenced Judaism during the Second Temple period. Citation: Hengel, Martin. *Judaism and Hellenism: Studies in their Encounter in Palestine during the Early Hellenistic Period*. Fortress Press, 1974. - Gershom Scholem maintains that while Kabbalah developed within Jewish tradition, it may have assimilated external philosophical concepts. Citation: Scholem, Gershom. *Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism*. Schocken Books, 1941. - Peter Schäfer explores the possibility of cross-cultural influences on early Jewish mysticism. Citation: Schäfer, Peter. *The Origins of Jewish Mysticism*. Princeton University Press, 2009. #### Arguments for Originality - Unique Symbolism and Interpretation: - While there are overlaps in dream symbolism, many interpretations are specific to each tradition's religious and cultural narratives. - Distinct Methodologies: - Greek Tradition: Often focuses on empirical observations and categorization (e.g., Artemidorus). - Jewish Tradition: Emphasizes the role of divine will and the importance of righteousness in interpreting dreams. #### Arguments for Dependence - Shared Human Experiences: - Similarities may arise from common human experiences with dreams rather than direct borrowing. - Possible Philosophical Influence: - Jewish philosophers like Philo of Alexandria attempted to harmonize Jewish theology with Greek philosophy, which could have facilitated the integration of certain ideas. --- ### Conclusion The traditions of dream interpretation in ancient Greek and Jewish cultures exhibit both similarities and differences. While there are parallels in viewing dreams as meaningful and in the use of symbolism, each tradition approaches the subject from its unique worldview. The Greek tradition blends mythology with philosophical inquiry, often scrutinizing dreams' reliability. In contrast, the Talmudic and Kabbalistic perspectives integrate dreams more fully into their spiritual and religious frameworks, considering them vital for understanding divine messages and personal growth. The question of originality versus dependence remains complex. While cultural exchanges likely occurred, the core principles and methods of dream interpretation in each tradition suggest largely independent development influenced by their respective religious and philosophical foundations. --- ### References Primary Greek Sources: - Homer - *The Odyssey*, trans.
