The document titled "Albrecht Dürer und die 'Hypnerotomachia Poliphili'" by Georg Leidinger is a 1929 lecture publication examining the provenance and intellectual significance of a copy of the Hypnerotomachia Poliphili—a renowned Renaissance text—that appears to have belonged to Albrecht Dürer. Leidinger presents the discovery of a purchase note in a library copy of the Hypnerotomachia and builds a case for its authenticity and relevance to Dürer's life, library, and intellectual interests, particularly in the context of Renaissance humanism and symbolic interpretation (Hieroglyphenkunde).

1. Introduction and Discovery of the Dürer Provenance


Leidinger recounts how he discovered a note in a copy of the Hypnerotomachia Poliphili at the Bayerische Staatsbibliothek in Munich. The note, dated 13 August 1555 and written by Dr. Erasmus Hock, states that the book was purchased from the library of “Albertus Dyrerus” (i.e., Albrecht Dürer). Leidinger examines whether this could plausibly refer to the famous artist and ultimately concludes affirmatively based on orthographic conventions of the time and the unique manner of reference.

Key point: The pages with the provenance note were bound at the end of the book, which may explain why they had escaped previous notice【5†source】.

2. Historical Context of Dürer’s Library and Its Dispersal


Leidinger reconstructs the history of Dürer’s possessions after his death in 1528. His estate passed to his brother Andreas Dürer, who died in April 1555—just months before the book was acquired by Hock. This timeline strongly supports the hypothesis that the book had indeed belonged to Dürer and was sold after Andreas’s death.

Leidinger references a manuscript note by Johann Gabriel Doppelmayr, preserved in Nürnberg, which describes the inheritance path of Dürer's estate【5†source】.

3. Dürer’s Relationship to Books and Bibliophilic Activity


Dürer was closely linked to the world of books through his godfather Anton Koberger, a major printer. He became a book and art dealer and published major works like the Apocalypse (1498). Despite this, virtually no books from Dürer's personal library were previously known to survive with proven provenance.

Important example: Dürer owned a 1505 edition of Euclid printed in Venice, held today in Wolfenbüttel, and signed it himself【5†source】.

4. Dürer’s Recorded Book Purchases


Drawing from Dürer’s travel diary (notably his trip to the Netherlands in 1520-21), Leidinger catalogs his purchases of popular books including Lutheran tracts, Till Eulenspiegel, and Imperial festivities literature, illustrating Dürer’s active interest in printed matter.

Dürer also maintained a handwritten list of 16 works by Luther preserved in the British Museum【5†source】.

5. The Hypnerotomachia Poliphili


This Renaissance novel, printed by Aldus Manutius in 1499, is a dream narrative rich in classical allusions, mysterious architecture, and hieroglyphic emblems. It is attributed to the Dominican monk Francesco Colonna.

The woodcut illustrations and typographic design make it a pinnacle of early printing, admired for its artistry as much as its literary content【5†source】.

6. The Humanist Symbolism and Hieroglyphic Culture


Leidinger explores how Hypnerotomachia belongs to the broader tradition of Renaissance Hieroglyphenkunde—the study and invention of pseudo-Egyptian symbols. He cites Karl Giehlow’s foundational research, which traced this symbolic movement to the rediscovery of the Hieroglyphica of Horapollon, a late antique text that deeply influenced Renaissance allegory.

Giehlow compares this rediscovery to the later discovery of the Rosetta Stone, underscoring its role in reimagining symbolic language during the Renaissance【5†source】.

7. Dürer’s Interest in Hieroglyphs and Symbolism


The lecture draws parallels between the Hypnerotomachia and Dürer's known engagement with hieroglyphic language. Dürer collaborated with Willibald Pirckheimer, who translated Horapollon. A manuscript copy of Pirckheimer’s translation illustrated by Dürer survives (Cod. 3255, Vienna National Library). Dürer's illustrations in the Maximilian Prayer Book also incorporate Horapollonic hieroglyphs.

Leidinger lists 19 instances where Dürer used specific symbols (e.g., hare, crane, lion, stork, tortoise) that correspond to known hieroglyphic meanings from Horapollon, substantiating Dürer’s symbolic literacy【5†source】.

8. Visual Analysis of Dürer's Marginal Drawings


A thorough iconographic survey of the Gebetbuch Kaiser Maximilians, previously published by Leidinger, is presented as evidence of Dürer’s deep understanding of humanist symbolism. He correlates Dürer’s marginalia with specific Horapollonic interpretations.

Example: A hare symbolizing attentiveness, crane for vigilance, pelican for sacrificial love, goat for male generative power—all grounded in Horapollon's entries【5†source】.

9. Final Reflections on Dürer’s Ownership and Intellectual World


Leidinger closes by asserting that it is both plausible and culturally significant that Dürer owned the Hypnerotomachia. The book’s dense classical references, Renaissance symbolism, and dreamlike visuality would have appealed to Dürer’s sensibilities, both as an artist and as a scholar deeply embedded in Renaissance humanism.

The woodcuts of the Hypnerotomachia and Dürer’s own symbolic language can be viewed as part of a broader Renaissance synthesis of art, mysticism, and classical revival.

Conclusion


Leidinger’s study is both a detective story and an intellectual reconstruction. It proposes that Dürer not only owned a copy of the Hypnerotomachia Poliphili but also that this ownership fits seamlessly into what we know of his bibliophilia, humanist network, and symbolic imagination. Georg Leidinger's 1929 lecture Albrecht Dürer und die “Hypnerotomachia Poliphili” presents a scholarly, archival, and iconographic argument for Albrecht Dürer’s ownership of a particular copy of the Hypnerotomachia Poliphili, the 1499 Venetian typographic masterpiece printed by Aldus Manutius. The argument is built from multiple converging lines of evidence: a provenance inscription, historical inheritance data, bibliophilic context, intellectual context, and art-historical symbolic parallels. Below is a detailed analysis and critical evaluation of this argument.

I. THE CENTRAL CLAIM

Leidinger’s central thesis is that a copy of the Hypnerotomachia Poliphili held at the Bayerische Staatsbibliothek (BSB) in Munich once belonged to Albrecht Dürer. The foundation of this claim is a provenance inscription on one of the flyleaves:

Inscription (translated from Latin/German):

"This book, Poliphilus, was bought by me, Doctor Erasmus Hock, on August 13, 1555, from the library of the famous painter Albertus Dyrerus."

The spelling “Albertus Dyrerus” is consistent with Latinized orthographies of the period and plausible for Dürer.

II. ANALYSIS OF THE COPY ITSELF

A. Provenance Inscription

  • Written by Dr. Erasmus Hock, a known humanist physician and book collector.

  • Dated August 13, 1555.

  • Notes that the book was acquired “from the library of Albertus Dyrerus.”


Credibility of Hock: Leidinger notes that Hock is not known to have engaged in forgery or exaggeration, and the precision of the date and style of Latin suggest a sincere record-keeping entry.

B. Physical Aspects and Binding

  • The inscription appears on an added flyleaf at the end of the book—bound in later, explaining its obscurity to earlier cataloguers.

  • The copy is a typical Aldine folio, printed in 1499, with the original page layout and typeface, but bound in a later cover (likely mid-16th century).


The Aldine edition was printed in Venice, the same city where Dürer was in 1505, and where he purchased the Euclid now known to survive in Wolfenbüttel.

C. Marginalia

  • Leidinger explicitly notes the absence of handwritten annotations by Dürer or anyone else in the margins of the book. This is a major caveat.

  • The copy does not bear a signature by Dürer, unlike his Euclid, which he signed.


Nonetheless, Leidinger argues that this silence is not unusual for Dürer’s generation, especially in aesthetic or symbolic books.

III. HISTORICAL AND BIOGRAPHICAL CONTEXT

A. Chain of Possession

  • Dürer died in 1528, leaving his possessions to his wife and then to his brother Andreas Dürer.

  • Andreas died in April 1555, four months before Hock’s purchase in August 1555.

  • Hock’s note thus perfectly aligns with a possible estate sale following Andreas’s death.


This timeline adds significant plausibility. The name, date, and city all align with known figures and events.

IV. INTELLECTUAL CONTEXT

A. Dürer’s Bibliophilia

  • Dürer was the godson of Anton Koberger, a major printer.

  • Acted as a book and print dealer, and printed his own works (e.g., the Apocalypse).

  • Purchased Till Eulenspiegel, Martin Luther tracts, and official imperial ceremony books during his travels.


B. Parallel Ownership: The Euclid Example

  • Dürer’s ownership of a Euclid printed in Venice is confirmed by his own signature.

  • This provides precedent for Dürer owning Venetian mathematical and symbolic texts, aligning with the Hypnerotomachia’s profile.


V. SYMBOLIC AND AESTHETIC FIT

Leidinger devotes a major portion of his argument to symbolic resonance:

A. Renaissance Hieroglyphic Studies


  • The Hypnerotomachia is steeped in allegory, pseudo-Egyptian hieroglyphs, and Neoplatonic symbolism.

  • Dürer owned or had access to Horapollon's Hieroglyphica, translated by his friend Willibald Pirckheimer.

  • Dürer illustrated 19 hieroglyphs in the Gebetbuch Kaiser Maximilians, interpreted through Horapollonic frameworks:

- Hare = attentiveness
- Crane = vigilance
- Lion = royalty
- Stork = filial piety
- Tortoise = silence

Leidinger demonstrates that Dürer did not merely borrow symbols for decoration but understood and used them as signs in a philosophical sense, as the Hypnerotomachia does.

B. Visual and Intellectual Kinship


  • The woodcuts in Hypnerotomachia and Dürer’s prints share:

- A deep engagement with classical architecture
- Vitruvian ideals
- A similar precision in composition
- Symbolic dreamscapes—compare Dürer’s Melencolia I to Poliphilo’s visions

VI. CRITICAL EVALUATION

A. Strengths of the Argument

1. Date and Name Match
- The inscription aligns exactly with the date Dürer's brother’s possessions would be sold.
- “Albertus Dyrerus” is the appropriate Latinized form.

2. Humanist Networks
- Hock, Pirckheimer, and Dürer all belonged to German-Venetian humanist circles.
- The book’s symbolic content fits Dürer’s documented artistic and philosophical interests.

3. Material Plausibility
- Dürer was in Venice in 1505 and likely bought rare books there.

4. Comparative Example
- Dürer’s Euclid provides precedent for his ownership of Venetian scholarly texts.

B. Weaknesses and Limitations

1. Lack of Direct Handwriting
- No autograph, signature, or annotation by Dürer is present.
- No indication he actually read or used this book.

2. Secondhand Inscription
- The claim rests entirely on Hock’s note; while credible, it is not direct evidence.

3. Book’s Silence
- The absence of marginalia leaves the depth of engagement unknown.

VII. CONCLUSION

Leidinger's argument is strongly circumstantial but highly plausible. It does not offer direct proof—like a signed bookplate or marginal notes by Dürer—but the convergence of:

  • Precise timing (Andreas Dürer’s death in 1555),

  • A credible buyer (Erasmus Hock),

  • The book’s Venetian origin,

  • Dürer’s known interests in symbolism and geometry,

  • Parallels in visual language and symbolic structure,


…makes the case highly compelling. In modern terms, this would be seen as a well-supported attribution with high probability, though not absolute certainty.

In sum, this copy of the Hypnerotomachia Poliphili sits not only within the physical world of Dürer’s time but in the intellectual world of his mind. Leidinger’s study turns this volume from a rare book into a key to Dürer’s symbolic imagination.
