ABSTRACT OF THE DISSERTATION

Alberti Before Florence: Early Sources Informing Leon Battista Alberti’s De Pictura

By

Peter Francis Weller

Doctor of Philosophy in Art History

University of California, Los Angeles, 2014

Professor Charlene Villaseñor Black, Chair

De pictura by Leon Battista Alberti (1404?-1472) is the earliest surviving treatise on

visual art written in humanist Latin by an ostensible practitioner of painting. The book

represents a definitive moment of cohesion between the two most conspicuous cultural

developments of the early Renaissance, namely, humanism and the visual arts. This dissertation

reconstructs the intellectual and visual environments in which Alberti moved before he entered

Florence in the curia of Pope Eugenius IV in 1434, one year before the recorded date of

completion of De pictura. For the two decades prior to his arrival in Florence, from 1414 to

1434, Alberti resided in Padua, Bologna, and Rome. Examination of specific textual and visual

material in those cities – sources germane to Alberti’s humanist and visual development, and

thus to the ideas put forth in De pictura – has been insubstantial. This dissertation will therefore

present an investigation into the sources available to Alberti in Padua, Bologna and Rome, and

will argue that this material helped to shape the prescriptions in Alberti’s canonical Renaissance

tract. By more fully accounting for his intellectual and artistic progression before his arrival in

Florence, this forensic reconstruction aims to fill a gap in our knowledge of Alberti’s formative

years and thereby underline impact of his early career upon his development as an art theorist.

Peter Francis Weller’s 2014 UCLA dissertation “Alberti Before Florence: Early Sources Informing Leon Battista Alberti’s _De pictura_” reorients the history of Alberti’s first art-theory treatise away from Florence toward the intellectual and visual environments of Padua, Bologna, and Rome between 1414 and 1434. His central claim is that the conceptual framework of _De pictura_—its vocabulary of perspective, _historia_, and imitation—was forged during these twenty years, before Alberti entered the Florentine circle of Brunelleschi and Donatello. By reconstructing the humanist and artistic milieus of those earlier cities, Weller argues that Alberti’s synthesis of humanism and the visual arts was grounded in northern-Italian rhetorical education, mathematical science, and the study of antiquity, not in direct contact with Florentine painters alone【7†Alberti_Before_Florence_Early.pdf†L1-L7】.

1 Argument and Methodological Aim


Weller adopts a forensic, contextual method joining intellectual and visual art history.
He contests the long-standing “Florentine genesis” narrative—articulated by Kenneth Clark and others—that treats _De pictura_ (1435–36) as the fruit of a single year’s exposure to Florentine artists. Following scholars such as Cecil Grayson, Anthony Grafton, and Francesco Furlan, he insists that Alberti’s Latin text preceded the vernacular version and was written for a learned audience to elevate painting to the rank of the liberal arts. The dissertation therefore maps Alberti’s pre-Florentine education and observation of artworks city by city, pairing textual sources (ancient, medieval, and humanist) with local monuments, manuscripts, and paintings that could have supplied examples for his theory of vision, proportion, and _historia_【7†Alberti_Before_Florence_Early.pdf†L1-L7】.

2 Padua (Chs. 1–3) — Humanist and Visual Foundations


Padua forms the core of Alberti’s early formation.
  • Intellectual background: After Petrarch’s return to Padua, the Carrara court fostered a “studia humanitatis” milieu centered on rhetoric and moral philosophy. Teachers like Pier Paolo Vergerio and Gasparino Barzizza shaped a curriculum stressing inventio, imitatio, and civic virtue—concepts that reappear in Alberti’s prescriptions for painters as moral rhetoricians.

  • Key mentors: Barzizza’s grammar school and Guarino da Verona’s teaching of Lucian introduced Alberti to classical models of eloquence and satire; Vittorino da Feltre’s integration of science and ethics modeled the union of mathematics and virtue that underlies Alberti’s geometry of perspective.

  • Visual environment: Weller links Alberti’s awareness of spatial construction and moral narrative to Giotto’s fresco cycles in the Cappella Scrovegni and to Altichiero da Zevio’s and Giusto de Menabuoi’s court commissions for the Carrara. These works’ organization of space and exempla of civic heroism prefigure Alberti’s doctrine that painting must move both “the eyes and the minds of spectators.”

  • Antique stimuli: Padua’s Roman sarcophagi and altars, collected by scholars such as Giovanni Dondi dell’Orologio, provided models for Alberti’s concern with relief, proportion, and inscription.


3 Bologna (Ch. 4) — Scientific and Northern Currents


While studying at the University of Bologna (c. 1421–28), Alberti encountered:
  • Mathematics and optics in the quadrivium, including medieval treatments of vision by Roger Bacon and Giovanni di Pelacani, informing his geometrical account of perspective.

  • Humanist historiography and rhetoric that trained him to construct _De pictura_ as a Latin dialogue among the liberal arts.

  • Visual culture: the sculptural programs of Nicola Pisano and Jacopo della Quercia at San Petronio, whose classicizing reliefs bridged Gothic narrative and antique form. Bologna also transmitted Netherlandish influence through manuscript illumination and knowledge of Jan van Eyck’s naturalism, enriching Alberti’s ideas of truthful depiction and portraiture.


4 Rome (Ch. 5) — Antiquity, _Materia_, and _Historia_


In the papal curia (1431–34) Alberti entered a laboratory of archaeology and restoration. Weller reconstructs his exposure to:
  • Ancient monuments—arches, sarcophagi, and the equestrian Marcus Aurelius—which furnished empirical data for his measurements and his ideal of the heroic nude.

  • Early Christian and Trecento art, especially Pietro Cavallini’s mosaics and frescoes, as precedents for Alberti’s concept of _historia_ as a unified, emotionally ordered composition.

  • Contemporary artists in Rome, including Masolino, Masaccio, Donatello, Gentile da Fabriano, and Pisanello, whose Roman works provided living demonstrations of antique revival and perspective before Alberti ever saw Florence.


5 Synthesis and Contribution


Across these case studies Weller demonstrates that:
  • Alberti’s Latin De pictura emerged from two intertwined traditions – the rhetorical ethics of Padua and the scientific classicism of Bologna and Rome.

  • His notion of the painter as philosopher-mathematician descends from humanist pedagogy (Cicero, Quintilian, Barzizza) combined with optical science and archaeological observation.

  • The treatise’s famous triad—circumscription, composition, and reception of light—thus crystallizes decades of education rather than a single Florentine epiphany.

Yes — and that’s a fascinating bridge to make. Peter Francis Weller’s Alberti Before Florence (2014) and Furnace and Fugue (Brown University, 2020) both model ways of reading interdisciplinary, multimedia artifacts that unify word, image, number, and moral philosophy.

When you set Weller’s reconstruction of Leon Battista Alberti’s intellectual world beside the digital study of 17th-century alchemical emblems like Atalanta Fugiens, several deep continuities appear.
Here’s how the twenty “takeaways” from Furnace and Fugue can be re-anchored in Weller’s humanist framework.

🧱 1. Image–Text–Music as Humanist Unity


Weller shows that Alberti’s De pictura joins visual art to rhetoric and geometry — a triple art integrating eye, word, and number.
→ 17th-century alchemical emblems like Maier’s extend that same ideal into image-text-sound: engraving + epigram + fugue. Both embody a Renaissance conviction that knowledge is multisensory harmony.

🪶 2. Emblems as Moral Rhetoric


Alberti made painting a branch of eloquence; each historia must persuade ethically.
→ Maier’s emblems function similarly: each emblem is a moralized allegory of transformation. The alchemist’s task parallels Alberti’s painter — to move the viewer’s soul toward virtue through symbolic action.

🏛️ 3. The Artist/Alchemist as Philosopher


Weller emphasizes Alberti’s invention of the learned artist — trained in geometry, rhetoric, and virtue.
→ The emblem tradition preserves this model: Maier the physician-musician-philosopher is the 17th-century heir of Alberti’s uomo universale ideal, combining scientific experiment and spiritual vision.

🔭 4. Geometry, Optics, and Proportion


For Weller, Alberti’s grounding in Paduan optics and Bolognese mathematics produced De pictura’s perspectival system.
→ Alchemical engravers similarly use geometric layouts — circles, pyramids, vessels — to visualize cosmic proportion. Their designs translate Alberti’s spatial perspective into symbolic perspective, mapping mental rather than optical depth.

🔤 5. The Humanist Commentary Tradition


Weller situates Alberti amid the studia humanitatis, reading classical texts as living models for modern creation.
→ The emblem authors inherit that exegetical habit: they build glossed images and captions as commentaries on nature. Reading emblems through Weller’s lens means treating them as visual commentaries in the humanist sense.

⚗️ 6. Materiality and Medium


Weller stresses that Alberti’s ideas grew out of manuscript and architectural practices in Padua and Rome.
→ Furnace and Fugue highlights the material operations of print and performance. Both imply that medium matters — whether pigment on wall or copperplate with music, form shapes meaning.

🎓 7. Education and the Liberal Arts


Weller reconstructs Alberti’s education through grammar, rhetoric, geometry, and moral philosophy.
→ Maier’s Atalanta Fugiens assumes the same encyclopedic training. Each emblem synthesizes music (quadrivium) and poetry (trivium): the complete liberal education embodied visually.

🧬 8. Allegory as Scientific Language


Weller interprets Alberti’s geometry and rhetoric as tools for reasoning about perception — a proto-scientific allegory.
→ Seventeenth-century alchemical imagery similarly encodes empirical processes (calcination, distillation) in mythic allegory. Both use allegory to translate experiment into meaning.

🕍 9. Antiquity Reborn through Medium


In De pictura Alberti re-engineers classical ideals (Cicero, Pliny) in new media (painting, treatise).
→ Maier re-engineers Ovid’s Atalanta myth through emblem and fugue. Both show how early modern authors used classical myth to structure experiential knowledge.

🎵 10. Proportion as Music of the Spheres


Alberti’s proportional systems align with musical ratios — a moral and cosmic harmony.
→ In Atalanta Fugiens the fugues literalize that harmony: musical proportion becomes audible alchemy. The emblem’s soundscape realizes Alberti’s theoretical “geometry of grace.”

📜 11. The Reader/Viewer as Participant


Weller’s Alberti treats the viewer as an interpreter who must read a painting’s moral narrative.
→ Maier’s reader-singer plays or sings the fugue, embodying transformation. Both envision knowledge as participatory performance.

🧭 12. Cosmology and Civic Order


For Alberti, perspective mirrored civic and cosmic order: the painting’s geometry reflects divine reason.
→ In Maier, alchemical correspondences (planets, metals) express that same cosmic law. The emblematic cosmos is an echo of Alberti’s architectural universe.

📖 13. The Scholarly Treatise and the Emblem Book


De pictura becomes a model of scholarly form — learned, systematic, Latinate.
→ Emblem books translate that mode into mixed-media pedagogy. Both are didactic systems meant to teach how to see, read, and think simultaneously.

🔥 14. Transformation as Epistemology


Weller reads Alberti’s art theory as a metaphor for the transformation of sight into knowledge.
→ In alchemy, transformation is literal and spiritual. The emblem thus extends Alberti’s epistemology: to see rightly is to transmute perception into wisdom.

🧫 15. The Laboratory and the Studio


Padua’s medical and optical studies, as Weller shows, shaped Alberti’s concern with observation.
→ The alchemical workshop mirrors the painter’s studio — both spaces where material experiment meets intellectual speculation.

🏗️ 16. Architecture and Emblematic Composition


Alberti’s architectural writings treat buildings as constructed arguments.
→ The emblem page likewise functions architectonically: frame, text block, and musical stave create an ordered visual architecture for meaning.

🕯️ 17. From Scholastic Commentary to Interactive Media


Weller’s contextual method relies on reading manuscript glosses and marginalia as evidence of learning in practice.
→ Furnace and Fugue’s digital interface revives that interactivity: zooming, annotating, playing. Both invite readers to act as co-interpreters, not passive consumers.

⚖️ 18. Virtue, Decorum, and Discipline


Alberti’s painter must exercise moderation and moral order.
→ The alchemist’s emblem dramatizes ethical discipline — patience, humility, purity — as prerequisites for success. Each medium moralizes its craft.

🪞 19. Reflection and Self-Knowledge


Alberti defines painting as a mirror of nature and of the soul.
→ Maier’s Atalanta emblems literalize reflection (mirrors, vessels, water) as metaphors for self-knowledge and divine image. Both use reflection to join epistemology and spirituality.

🌍 20. Continuity of Humanist Cosmopoetics


Ultimately Weller’s thesis reveals a pre-Florentine pan-Italian humanism where art, science, and ethics converge.
→ Seventeenth-century alchemical emblems extend that humanist cosmopoetics into the Baroque: a world still convinced that material art can image divine order.

✳️ In short


Weller’s Alberti offers the Renaissance blueprint for what Furnace and Fugue demonstrates in practice:
  • Art as scientia (structured knowledge).

  • Image as argument.

  • Viewer as participant.


Thus, to study 17th-century alchemical emblems through Weller’s lens is to trace how the Alberti-type humanist synthesis of geometry, rhetoric, and morality survives into the Hermetic multimedia art of the early modern age.

2. REGRESO A LA PINTURA POR EL VERSO

Estas circunstancias biográficas son importantes para comprender
lo que significa el libro A la pintura2. De entre los muchos caminos de ida
y vuelta que Rafael Alberti emprende en su vida a golpe siempre de unas
circunstancias históricas atroces tanto en España como en Europa, A la
pintura se convierte en uno de los caminos de regreso que le es dado al
poeta por el verso. El libro en su conjunto es un homenaje a tanta belleza
contenida en los límites de los lienzos o de las pinturas murales o de los
frescos; homenaje sostenido a través de un ejercicio de écfrasis arrebatado
de entusiasmo plástico y verbal. Alberti se aplica a ensayar los principios
de la écfrasis –Ut pictura poesis– resolviendo en palabra poética tantas
y tantas imágenes pictóricas acumuladas por el tiempo y guardadas en
su retina (González, 1990). Pero más allá, tal como ha sido reiterado por
la crítica, en 1945 –es decir, al terminar la II Guerra Mundial y como

hizo volver a la experimentación de los colores y la línea, pero esta vez
entremezclándolos con la palabra, es decir, con el verso” (Alberti, 1987:

D. R. Edward Wright

T HE De pictura has been the focus of numerous studies, but it remains unclear exactly
what sort of work Alberti composed in I435 and what its intended purpose was, for
scholars have over the years perceived it differently. It has been called a scientific

treatise, an essay, a beginner's handbook, and even a Ciceronian oration. Those
interested in its mathematical content most commonly refer to it as 'the first modern
treatise on the theory of painting',' or 'a fully fledged treatise on vanishing point
perspective'.2 On the other hand, in writings which deal with the more literary aspects of
De pictura it is sometimes termed an 'essay': 'Alberti was not in the least concerned with
the formulation of a science of vision.

The main thesis of the essay is the creation of a

communicatory theory of the arts, heavily based on the humanistic concept of the
istoria... [as] a means of moral edification'.3

a. A preliminary definition of the art, describing its goals and scope;

but rather secundum ordinem naturae. Book I introduces the young boy to very simple, easily
memorized axioms and elements which will prepare him for the study of painting proper.
Book II presents the adolescent workshop trainee with a survey of the branches of painting
he must master before undertaking a professional career. Book IIi advises the adult

practitioner what steps he may take to become an ideal or perfect painter. The pedagogic
model chosen by Alberti was purposely geared to the several stages of human mental

development. It began with basic elements, the ABCs, and built toward more and more
