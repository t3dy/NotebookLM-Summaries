What Is Event Cognition?

Event cognition is the way our brains perceive, segment, interpret, and remember ‚Äúevents‚Äù ‚Äî basically, meaningful happenings in time. It‚Äôs how we make sense of the flow of experience, turning raw sensory data into structured, meaningful episodes.

  • An event could be anything from seeing a sunset, hearing a story, having a religious vision, or undergoing a ritual.

  • Cognitively, events are chunks of experience our mind organizes ‚Äî with beginnings, middles, and ends.

  • Our brains constantly predict and update what‚Äôs happening, and this helps us segment continuous experience into discrete events.


Why Does Event Cognition Matter for Religious and Visionary Experience?

  • Religious experiences (like visions, revelations, mystical encounters) are often rare and impactful events.

  • Event cognition provides a framework to study how people recognize, interpret, and remember these experiences.

  • It highlights the interaction between:

- The sensory and bodily experience itself,
- The cognitive processes that structure experience,
- And the cultural/religious schemas people use to interpret it.

Key Cognitive Science Concepts Relevant to Event Cognition

1. Predictive Processing

  • The brain constantly makes predictions about what it will perceive next based on prior experience.

  • When actual sensory input matches predictions, experience feels smooth and ordinary.

  • When input violates predictions, we experience surprise or salience, which grabs attention.

  • Visionary or religious events often involve prediction errors ‚Äî something unexpected or extraordinary happens that doesn't fit existing models.

  • This surprise can make an event feel more meaningful or sacred.


2. Schemas and Scripts

  • Schemas are mental frameworks or knowledge structures that help us interpret incoming information.

  • Scripts are schemas about the typical sequence of events (e.g., what normally happens at a wedding).

  • Religious traditions provide schemas/scripts for sacred events ‚Äî like how to interpret a vision or a divine message.

  • When an event fits a schema, it is easily integrated; when it does not, it may be considered extraordinary or revelatory.


3. Event Segmentation

  • Our brains automatically divide continuous experience into discrete events.

  • This segmentation is crucial for memory encoding ‚Äî we tend to remember events, not streams.

  • Segmentation is triggered by changes in context, action, or goals.

  • In religious contexts, the moment a vision appears may mark a new event boundary, signaling its importance.


4. Attention and Salience

  • Attention selects which parts of experience get processed deeply.

  • Events that are emotionally intense, surprising, or culturally meaningful capture more attention.

  • This increased attention enhances encoding and later recall, explaining why visionary events are vividly remembered.


5. Emotion and Affect

  • Emotion colors event encoding and interpretation.

  • Strong feelings (awe, fear, ecstasy) linked to visionary experiences reinforce their significance.

  • Cognitive appraisal theories explain how we evaluate events emotionally based on their meaning for us.


6. Memory and Narrative Construction

  • Memory of events is reconstructive, shaped by later reflection and cultural storytelling.

  • People often frame visionary experiences into narratives that fit their religious tradition.

  • Over time, memory and interpretation co-evolve, which can reinforce the sacredness of the event.


Putting It All Together: Ann Taves‚Äô Use of Event Cognition

Taves builds on these concepts to show:

  • Religious/visionary experiences are special kinds of events characterized by predictive surprise and strong affective valence.

  • They become ‚Äúreligious‚Äù when interpreted through cultural/religious schemas that assign sacred significance.

  • By understanding how people cognitively process and structure these events, we can better explain why and how religious experiences happen, and why they have lasting impact.

  • Event cognition helps bridge psychology, neuroscience, and cultural studies in the study of religion.


Additional Concepts Worth Knowing

  • Phenomenology: The study of lived experience, important for understanding how events feel from the inside.

  • Embodiment: Many religious experiences involve bodily sensations (e.g., trance, ecstasy) ‚Äî event cognition includes how body signals contribute to event perception.

  • Attention Network Theory: Different brain networks govern alerting, orienting, and executive control of attention, all relevant to processing meaningful events.
