Giordano Bruno’s contact with alchemy primarily stemmed from his time in Naples and his interactions with intellectuals and esoteric traditions throughout Europe. In Naples, he was exposed to alchemical and hermetic knowledge through secret academies, such as those founded by Girolamo Ruscelli and Giovan Battista Della Porta. These academies experimented with alchemical recipes and theories, contributing to the intellectual culture that shaped Bruno’s early philosophical outlook. He also likely accessed prohibited esoteric texts in the library of the S. Domenico Maggiore convent, which housed works by Paracelsus, Cornelius Agrippa, and Hermes Trismegistus. These influences introduced him to themes of alchemical transmutation, macrocosm-microcosm theories, and the symbolic language of alchemy. Bruno’s contact with alchemy expanded during his travels in Germany, where he interacted with alchemical circles influenced by Paracelsus. A significant figure in this context was Raphael Egli, a Swiss theologian and alchemist. Egli invited Bruno to Zurich to lecture on Aristotelian terminology, and their interactions appear to have influenced Egli’s later writings. Egli authored numerous alchemical and apocalyptic texts, often under pseudonyms, such as Filippo di Gabala. He is credited with about 60 works, blending mystical and alchemical thought, and served as a bridge between German and Italian esoteric traditions. Egli’s ideas were shaped by his engagement with Bruno, and he incorporated some of Bruno’s cosmological and philosophical themes into Rosicrucian doctrines. Bruno’s macrocosm-microcosm theories and pantheistic leanings resonated with Egli’s work, making him a pivotal figure in adapting Bruno’s ideas within a Rosicrucian framework.
