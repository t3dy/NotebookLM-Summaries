1–10 Summaries of Lesser-Known Magical Sources in Thorndike

1. Liber Vaccae (“Book of the Cow”)


A bizarre Arabic–Latin collection of magical and pseudo-chemical procedures attributed to Pliny or “the Wise Men of India.” It contains recipes for artificial generation of life, transformations of animals, unguents that induce visions or madness, and processes resembling alchemy. Thorndike treats it not as nonsense but as evidence of medieval belief in experimental manipulation of nature, showing how magical handbooks encouraged readers to treat the natural world as malleable and subject to hidden laws. Its grotesqueness is less important to Thorndike than the mindset it cultivates: try this recipe and observe the result.

2. The Secreta Secretorum (technical/experimental sections)


Beyond its famous didactic advice to rulers, this text includes alchemical, talismanic, medical, and physiognomic experiments. Thorndike emphasizes that these practical portions circulated widely among Latin readers and may have been taken quite seriously by early experimenters. The text represents an early instance of “secret knowledge” as a technical discipline, blending astrology, chemistry, and political science. Thorndike sees it as a bridge from learned magical lore to more systematic observations in medicine and alchemy.

3. Pseudo-Democritus / Bolos of Mendes (Latin fragments of the Cheirokmeta)


Although known today mostly through Greek fragments, the Latin Middle Ages preserved snippets of material attributed to “Democritus the Magician.” These included recipes for dyes, hardening metals, manipulating precious stones, and performing feats of illusion. Thorndike uses this transmission to argue that medieval readers inherited a craft-based experimental tradition rooted in antiquity. Bolos’s willingness to investigate natural causes aligns strongly with Thorndike’s thesis that “experimental science” emerged from artisanship and magical operations rather than abstract philosophy.

4. “Alexander of Aphrodisias” Experimenta


A spurious but influential set of magical and medical recipes attributed to the Aristotelian commentator. These texts offer procedures for healing, enhancing memory, influencing emotions, and producing marvels. Thorndike seizes on this tradition to show how authoritative names legitimized experimentation. Medieval scholars often believed that Aristotle and his commentators had performed practical trials, blurring the line between philosophical credibility and magical experimentation. This created an intellectual climate willing to test recipes or trust others who claimed to.

5. Pre-Solomonic magical treatises (lesser Solomon books)


Aside from the later Clavicula Salomonis, the Middle Ages preserved a family of obscure Solomonic manuals concerned with astrological timings, incantations, summoning techniques, and construction of ritual tools. Thorndike notes that these manuscripts, often in marginal miscellanies, represent a long-lived tradition of ritualized procedural knowledge: step-by-step instructions governed by precise natural correspondences. For Thorndike, their importance lies not in demonology but in how they model method, precision, and repeatable protocol — ingredients of experimental practice.

6. Liber Prestigiorum Heremii (Book of the Illusions of Hermes)


This rare text attributes to Hermes a series of “prestiges,” meaning both illusions and technological marvels (e.g., fire tricks, chemical reactions, mechanical devices). Thorndike highlights it because it blurs magic and mechanical ingenuity, showing that medieval readers did not sharply divide natural magic from early engineering. The text exemplifies the medieval understanding that technology could create marvels that seemed magical, a conceptual bridge from occult lore to the mechanical arts later central to Renaissance experimental natural philosophy.

7. The “Thessalus” tradition (astrological–medical conjunction literature)


Attributed to the ancient physician Thessalus of Tralles, these works describe how planetary configurations influence the efficacy of medical treatments. Thorndike treats this as an important chapter in the history of medical astrology, a theory that demanded attention to observation, timing, and causality. Even though based in magical cosmology, the structure of Thessalian recipes encouraged medieval practitioners to attend to natural regularities. Thorndike argues that this sustained a proto-scientific habit of correlating natural conditions with outcomes.

8. Liber Antimaqui (Antimachean Book)


An obscure magical compilation dealing with sympathetic and apotropaic operations. Thorndike examines it as an example of technical magic embedded in moral or mythic frameworks, showing how medieval scribes often combined narrative elements with practical instructions. Its significance lies in demonstrating how magical handbooks circulated widely among clerics and were not isolated “occultist” artifacts. For Thorndike, every such text constituted a node in a broader intellectual network that treated hidden virtues and correspondences as legitimately investigable.

9. Manuscript-only talismanic tables in Picatrix


Thorndike combed through Latin manuscripts of the Picatrix to highlight sections never printed or translated in his day, especially the intricate astrological talisman tables. These tables systematize correspondences between planets, stones, plants, and metals with extraordinary rigor. Thorndike sees this as an early attempt to catalogue nature according to hidden causal laws. Even if modern readers classify this as superstition, Thorndike argues that the mindset is classificatory and quasi-scientific, showing an impulse toward comprehensive natural explanation.

10. Evax's Lapidary (gem-lore attributed to “Evax, King of Arabia”)


A lapidary promising miraculous effects from stones — protection, healing, influence, fortune. Thorndike uses texts like this to show that medieval Europe treated minerals as repositories of natural powers, encouraging empirical manipulation of stones (grinding, heating, engraving). Rather than dismissing this as credulous, Thorndike emphasizes that it shaped early chemical curiosity and craft experimentation. The lapidaries taught people to observe mineral behavior, anticipate properties, and catalog effects — a proto-mineralogy under the sign of magic.

11–20 Summaries of Lesser-Known Magical Sources in Thorndike

12. The Liber de Mirabilibus Mundi (falsely attributed to Albertus Magnus)


One of many “Books of Marvels” circulating under Albert’s name, usually including exotic natural wonders, magical stones, strange animals, and recipes for producing marvels. Thorndike treats these not as fantasies but as attempts to catalog possible operations of nature, often arranged in quasi-scientific categories. Even the more unlikely entries show a desire to understand unusual phenomena. The misattribution to Albertus heightened the book’s credibility and encouraged scholastic readers to integrate magical marvels into natural philosophy.

13. Anonymous Virtutes Herbarium texts (herb-magic manuals)


These early medieval compilations list plants with brief but potent claims about their hidden powers — curing diseases, warding spirits, influencing emotions, or inducing dreams. For Thorndike, such herbals are important because they represent practical, craft-based knowledge passed among monastic, medical, and artisanal circles. They encouraged empirical procedures: gathering herbs at certain times, preparing concoctions with precise steps, testing effects. Thorndike views them as an early form of experimental botany, albeit framed in magical categories.

14. Liber Imaginum attributed to Thābit ibn Qurra (Latin talismanic treatise)


A significant but under-read work in which astrological images — engraved on metals or stones at specific planetary hours — are said to exert influence on weather, health, and emotions. Thorndike stresses its importance because it shows systematization: rules for making talismans, conditions for success, materials required. Such texts modeled procedural exactness, a feature Thorndike sees as anticipatory of experimental method. They also reveal how Arabic astral magic infiltrated Western natural philosophy.

15. Circa Instans (early Salernitan medical-pharmaceutical manual)


Though it later became influential, Thorndike emphasizes its lesser-known aspects: pragmatic instructions for identifying, storing, and preparing materia medica. This is not “high magic,” but it inherits the same belief in occult properties, now framed in medical terms. Thorndike treats it as evidence that practical medicine and magical naturalism overlapped, and that empirically oriented pharmacy grew directly out of traditions that assumed nature’s hidden virtues were manipulable.

16. “Sphragistic” magic — the literature of seals and sigils


Not a single text, but a body of manuscript traditions concerned with engraved seals believed to channel cosmic forces. Thorndike highlights these because they required precision in engraving, timing, and material selection. Their logic — that nature responds consistently to the right combination of matter and moment — reflects a proto-experimental mindset. Such literature also shows the absorption of late antique Hermeticism into medieval technical practice.

17. The Cyranides (Greek–Byzantine magical natural history)


A compendium of correspondences among stones, animals, plants, and letters of the alphabet. Western readers encountered it in partial, often corrupt Latin versions. Thorndike uses this text to illustrate the continuity of symbolic-natural magic from antiquity to the Middle Ages. It is important for him because it presents nature as an interlocking system of sympathies: observing one property helps infer another. This holistic but orderly vision of natural powers became a substrate for later scientific classification.

18. Lesser-known astrological treatises of Michael Scot


Beyond his major translations, Michael Scot was credited with numerous shorter, technical works on astrological elections, weather prediction, and divinatory experiments. Thorndike often dwells on these obscure fragments, treating them as windows into the working notebooks of medieval astrologer-magicians. Because they were circulated as practical, use-oriented manuals, they reveal an applied astronomy that blurred into meteorology and protophysics. Thorndike sees this as “science in the making,” indistinguishable from magic yet committed to prediction grounded in celestial order.

19. The Arbatel de magia veterum


A 16th-century text, but Thorndike treats it as representative of an older magical rationalism. Unlike demonic grimoires, the Arbatel presents magic as a form of pious natural philosophy, emphasizing moral purity, disciplined study, and alignment with divine order. Thorndike sees it as the endpoint of a tradition in which magic becomes a spiritualized science of nature’s powers, illustrating how Renaissance thinkers reinterpreted magical practice as part of a lawful cosmos. It also contributed to the intellectual climate that nurtured early modern experimenters.

20. Liber Razielis (non-Kabbalistic sections)


This massive angelological encyclopedia contains elaborate cosmologies, lists of natural virtues, astrological correspondences, and ritual instructions. Thorndike is particularly interested in the sections that present natural powers and procedural recipes rather than overt mysticism. Its encyclopedic nature made it a reference manual for clerical experimenters who wanted to consult angelic intelligences or manipulate natural forces. Thorndike views it as evidence of how angelic cosmology, natural magic, and empirical practice existed in the same intellectual ecosystem.

18. The value of “minor” and weird texts

Thorndike:
By spending energy on Liber vaccae, obscure Solomonic manuals, talismanic tables, cosmetic books, and secret books, he implicitly argues that minor magical texts are structurally important, not just curiosities. citeturn1search29turn1search25

Later scholarship:
This has become standard; scholars now write whole monographs on things like Picatrix, Liber vaccae, ars notoria, or individual grimoires. Thorndike’s sense that the “weird little stuff” reveals the texture of actual practice is very much in line with current micro-historical and material-culture approaches. citeturn1search25turn1search21

19. Magic as a crossroads of disciplines

Thorndike:
Although he doesn’t use the language, his practice shows magic as a crossroads where medicine, astronomy, theology, philosophy, and artisanal crafts meet.

Later scholarship:
Kieckhefer literally describes magic as a “crossroads” of medieval culture, making explicit what Thorndike does implicitly. citeturn1search23turn1search35
Daston & Park similarly treat marvels as points where natural philosophy, theology, and courtly culture intersect. citeturn1search14turn1search18

20. Foundational but now “background infrastructure”

Thorndike:
His eight volumes are essentially a giant historiographical infrastructure project: map the terrain of texts, show that magic belongs in the history of science, hint at continuities, and leave reinterpretation to the future.

Later scholarship & controversies:
Modern debates about:

  • the “Scientific Revolution” vs. long continuities,

  • the status of alchemy and astrology,

  • how to define “esotericism,”

  • how to integrate social/gender/material histories—


all play out on a map Thorndike helped draw. Scholars now critique his progressivism, his narrow social lens, and his focus on Latin elites, but almost nobody goes back to the old position that magic is irrelevant to the history of science. On that core point, Thorndike “wins,” and the controversies are over how to tell that entangled story, not whether to tell it. citeturn1search17turn1search9

3. How This Fits Into Current Historiography

These topics sit at the intersection of several influential currents in current scholarship:

1. The “Material Turn”


Historians now treat instruments not as passive tools but as active epistemic agents.

2. The “Practice Turn” in history of science


Instead of focusing only on abstract theories, scholars examine what practitioners did with their hands.
→ Alchemical ovens, scrying mirrors, or astrological boards become evidence of experimental cultures.

3. Global history of science


Talismanic instruments in Islamic, Jewish, African, and Asian traditions contribute to a polycentric narrative of science, not a Eurocentric one.

4. Esotericism as a legitimate field


Work by Hanegraaff, Forshaw, and others treats magical technologies as part of intellectual history, not marginal curiosities.

5. Visual and sensory studies


Mirrors, diagrams, ritual spaces, and sensory manipulation techniques are being reinterpreted as designed media technologies for producing knowledge or visions.

6. History of engineering and mechanics
