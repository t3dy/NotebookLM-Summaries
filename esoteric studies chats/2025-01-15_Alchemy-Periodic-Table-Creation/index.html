Neon was completely unknown to alchemists. As an inert noble gas discovered in 1898 through spectroscopic analysis, neon did not interact chemically in ways that were relevant to alchemical practices focused on reactive and transformative substances.

Observed Properties and Limitations:
Neon’s colorless, odorless, and non-reactive nature meant it had no practical application or symbolic significance in alchemy. Alchemists did not have the means to isolate or detect such gases, and their laboratory practices did not involve the manipulation of noble gases.

Materia Prima:
Neon, being inert and non-reactive, did not fit into the alchemical framework that emphasized transformation and reactivity. It remained outside the scope of materia prima, which concentrated on mutable and purifiable substances.

Modern Perspective:
Neon is known for its use in lighting (neon signs), high-energy physics, and cryogenics. Its discovery expanded the understanding of noble gases and contributed to the development of the periodic table, but these advancements occurred long after the decline of alchemy.

Conclusion of First Ten Elements

The initial ten elements reveal a spectrum of interactions and recognitions within alchemical practices. While elements like mercury, sulfur, salt, gold, and silver were actively manipulated and studied for their transformative properties, others such as hydrogen and helium remained beyond the observational and theoretical capacities of alchemists. The distinction underscores the empirical limitations of alchemical science and sets the stage for the gradual transition to modern chemistry, where the identification and understanding of elements are grounded in atomic theory and systematic experimentation.

Logic of Periods (Rows)


In the periodic table, rows (periods) represent the progressive filling of electron shells in atoms, with each new period corresponding to a new principal energy level. Alchemically, this progression can be loosely related to increasing complexity in the properties of substances as they move from simpler, more fundamental materials to denser, more reactive metals.

Example from Period 1: Hydrogen and Helium


  • Hydrogen (H): Though unknown to alchemists, hydrogen can be conceptually linked to the idea of primal material or spiritual essence due to its simplicity and foundational role in chemical reactions. Alchemists may have unknowingly encountered hydrogen during reactions involving acids and metals (e.g., the evolution of hydrogen gas during dissolution of iron in hydrochloric acid) but lacked the tools to isolate or identify it.

  • Helium (He): Helium’s inert nature makes it inaccessible to alchemical thought, which focused on reactive and transformative substances. However, its position as the second simplest element reflects the progression from hydrogen's reactivity to helium’s stability, echoing alchemical notions of perfect stillness or "fixity."


Example from Period 3: Sodium to Argon


  • Sodium (Na): Representing a highly reactive alkali metal, sodium aligns with the alchemical fascination with salts and their reactivity. Sodium salts (e.g., sodium chloride) were widely used in purification processes, aligning with its reactive behavior.

  • Sulfur (S): Found in the same period, sulfur represents combustibility and volatility, symbolizing the fiery principle in alchemy. Alchemists extensively studied sulfur for its reactions with metals and its role in forming sulfides.

  • Argon (Ar): The inert nature of argon would be entirely foreign to alchemical practice, emphasizing the growing divergence between observable reactivity and the modern understanding of periodic properties.


Logic of Groups (Columns)


In the periodic table, columns (groups) represent elements with similar valence electron configurations, resulting in shared chemical properties. Alchemists often grouped materials based on their observed behaviors, such as combustibility, volatility, or metallic character, which loosely parallels the logic of modern groups.

Example from Group 1: Alkali Metals


  • Lithium (Li), Sodium (Na), Potassium (K): These elements were unknown to alchemists in their elemental form, but their compounds (e.g., potash for potassium) were widely used in metallurgical and glassmaking processes. The reactivity of alkali metals corresponds to the alchemical interest in substances that could dissolve, purify, or facilitate transformations.


Example from Group 16: Chalcogens


  • Oxygen (O), Sulfur (S): Sulfur, a chalcogen, was deeply integrated into alchemical theory as a principle of fire and transformation. Oxygen, though unrecognized, played a crucial role in calcination and combustion processes. Alchemists interpreted the oxidative reactions they observed without understanding the role of oxygen as an element.


Example from Group 18: Noble Gases


  • Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar): These elements’ lack of reactivity excluded them from alchemical consideration. Alchemy focused on substances that visibly transformed, meaning noble gases had no place in alchemical frameworks. This absence highlights the limitations of alchemical methods in identifying non-reactive elements.


Crossing Periods and Groups: Evolution of Properties


The periodic table organizes elements such that reactivity and other properties vary predictably across periods and down groups. Alchemists, despite lacking this framework, noticed trends in behavior and often grouped materials accordingly.

Reactivity Across a Period


As you move across a period:
  • Metals (e.g., sodium, magnesium) transition to nonmetals (e.g., sulfur, chlorine).

  • Alchemical Observations: Alchemists noted that metals like iron and copper were malleable and reactive, while nonmetals like sulfur and salts had distinct properties that complemented metallurgical processes.


Reactivity Down a Group


As you move down a group:
  • Reactivity generally increases for metals (e.g., lithium to cesium) and decreases for nonmetals (e.g., fluorine to iodine).

  • Alchemical Observations: Alchemists indirectly observed such trends when working with series of similar substances, such as different salts or oxides. For example, the use of progressively more reactive alkali salts in purification reflects their empirical recognition of such patterns.


Alchemical Analogies for Periodic Trends


1. Increasing Reactivity in Metals:
- Alchemists working with "base metals" like lead (Pb) and tin (Sn) observed their tendency to oxidize or combine with sulfur to form sulfides. This aligns with modern trends of increasing metallic reactivity down a group.

2. Transition from Metals to Nonmetals:
- Moving from gold and silver to sulfur and salt in alchemical texts reflects an implicit understanding of the transition from conductive, malleable substances to nonmetallic, reactive ones.

3. Stability of Noble Metals:
- Gold and silver were valued for their resistance to corrosion and tarnish, paralleling the modern understanding of their low reactivity. This stability was often interpreted symbolically as a sign of perfection or spiritual purity.

Conclusion: Alchemical Logic Meets Modern Chemistry


While alchemists lacked the theoretical tools to describe elements in terms of atomic structure or electron configuration, their empirical methods and symbolic groupings often paralleled the patterns we recognize in the periodic table. Periods reflect the increasing complexity of materials, which alchemists sometimes linked to their ability to transform substances. Groups echo the shared reactivity and observable properties of elements, which alchemists categorized based on their practical applications. This overlap highlights the continuity between alchemical traditions and the scientific rigor that followed in the development of the periodic table.
