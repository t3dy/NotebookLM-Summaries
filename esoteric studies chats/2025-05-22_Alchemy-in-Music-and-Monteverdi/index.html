Wood treatments for musical instruments in the 16th and 17th centuries were crucial to both the acoustics and longevity of the instruments. These treatments included careful selection of wood species, seasoning techniques, and various finishing or impregnation processes. Below is an overview of historical practices, focusing primarily on stringed and keyboard instruments like viols, lutes, violins, harpsichords, and early guitars.

1. Wood Selection

Instrument makers in the 16th–17th centuries (especially in Italy, Germany, and Flanders) chose woods based on their tonal properties:

InstrumentSoundboardBody/Back/SidesNeck/Fingerboard
Lutes/ViolsSpruce (light, resonant)Maple, yew, or sycamoreEbony or hardwood with veneers
HarpsichordsSpruce or firPoplar, cypress, or walnutOften local hardwoods
Violins (later 17c)SpruceMaple (flamed preferred)Maple, ebony (fingerboard)

2. Seasoning and Aging of Wood

  • Air-drying: Wood was typically air-dried (seasoned) for many years (5–20+), stored under shelter to avoid warping.

  • Desap (sap removal): Some makers (especially in Italy) sought to reduce sap content through long-term drying or soaking.

  • Thermal treatment: There is some evidence (e.g., in Cremonese violin lore) of heat or sun exposure to "harden" or pre-age wood, though this is debated.


3. Chemical Treatments

Some historical documents and recent scientific analyses suggest early instrument makers experimented with chemical pre-treatments:

  • Boiling/steaming: Used to leach resins and oils, stabilize the wood, and potentially lighten its weight.

  • Alum or potash solutions: May have been used to preserve or alter tone, especially in gutta-percha varnish-like processes.

  • Tannins and iron: In rare cases, darkened the wood or reacted with acids for aesthetic purposes.


4. Varnishes and Finishes

The finish was central to protecting wood, enhancing sound, and adding beauty. There are two broad types:

a. Oil Varnishes


  • Linseed or walnut oil mixed with resins (e.g., amber, copal, mastic, sandarac).

  • Slow drying, penetrative, enhancing resonance and depth of color.

  • Used in Italian violin making (e.g., Cremonese varnish school).


b. Spirit Varnishes


  • Alcohol as a solvent with resins like shellac, mastic, or benzoin.

  • Faster drying, more brittle; used more in later harpsichords and furniture.


5. Glue and Internal Treatments

  • Animal glue (hide or fish glue) was used extensively and subtly affected resonance and repairability.

  • Some reports suggest internal wood surfaces were treated with:

- Casein-based washes (protein from milk + lime): water-resistant and slightly acoustic-dampening.
- Wax or oil linings: in some keyboard instruments, to prevent warping and dust infiltration.

6. Case Study: Stradivari & Cremonese Makers

Recent CT scans and chemical analyses of 17th-century Cremonese instruments (like Stradivarius and Guarneri violins) suggest:

  • Use of borax, potassium silicates, and even formic acid–perhaps to inhibit mold or harden the wood.

  • Multilayered varnish systems, often with a mineral ground layer to reflect sound and enhance luminosity.

  • The legendary "Stradivari tone" may partially stem from these nuanced treatments.


7. Regional Variations

  • Italian luthiers (e.g., Amati, Stradivari): rich varnishes, seasoned spruce/maple, possible chemical wood treatments.

  • Flemish harpsichord makers (e.g., Ruckers family): thick spruce soundboards, brightly painted cases, simpler finishes.

  • English viol makers (e.g., Barak Norman): darker, more austere finishes, occasional use of native woods like sycamore or beech.
