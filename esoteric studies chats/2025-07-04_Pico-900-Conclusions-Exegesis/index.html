Here is a translation and philosophical exegesis of the eight annotated conclusions from Pico della Mirandola's Conclusiones secundum Adelandum Arabem, numbered I.21.1 through I.21.8. These statements are attributed to "Adelandus Arabus" (likely Adelard of Bath or an Arabizing pseudepigraphic source) and reflect a Neoplatonic and Avicennian-Arabic philosophical framework filtered through Renaissance syncretism.

I.21.1


Latin: Intellectus agens nihil est aliud quam pars animae quae sursum manet et non cadit.
Translation:
The agent intellect is nothing other than that part of the soul which remains above and does not fall.

Exegesis:
This evokes the Neoplatonic-Arabic doctrine of a dual soul: one part remains in the divine, unchanging realm (the pars quae sursum manet), while the lower soul is subject to change, sensation, and embodiment (pars quae cadit). The intellectus agens (agent intellect), a central idea in Aristotelian and Islamic philosophy (especially in Avicenna and Averroes), is that aspect of the soul which abstracts universal forms from sensible data. Pico here aligns the agent intellect with the higher, immutable part of the soul—implying that true intellection is a divine, stable act, untouched by the flux of the body and matter.

I.21.2


Latin: Anima habet apud se rerum species: et excitatur tantum ab extrinsecis rebus.
Translation:
The soul possesses within itself the forms of things, and it is only awakened by things external to it.

Exegesis:
This is a deeply Platonic notion, with echoes of St. Augustine and Islamic Neoplatonists like Avicenna. The soul already has the forms (species)—not as sensory images but as intelligible patterns. Experience or external objects don’t implant knowledge, but rather excite or awaken what is already within. This corresponds to the idea of anamnesis (recollection) in Plato and reflects the distinction between impressio (external stimulus) and expressio (inner recognition).

I.21.3


Latin: Ad complementum praecedentis conclusionis quam non solum Adelandus: sed omnes Mauri dicunt dico ego illas species actu et substantialiter esse in parte quae non cadit: et recipi de novo et accidentaliter in parte quae cadit.
Translation:
To complete the preceding conclusion, which not only Adelard but all the Moors [i.e., Muslim philosophers] affirm, I say that those forms exist actually and substantially in the part [of the soul] which does not fall; and are received anew and accidentally in the part which falls.

Exegesis:
Here Pico elaborates on the dualism of soul. The species—the intelligible forms—truly and substantially exist in the higher soul. When we experience things in the embodied condition (pars quae cadit), we receive them only accidentally (i.e., they appear fleetingly in imagination or sensation). This aligns with the Avicennian notion that the human soul contains all forms potentially and accesses them fully only in abstraction and intellectual purification. It also reflects the influence of Plotinus: the soul is divided between its eternal, intelligible self and its temporal, bodily projection.

I.21.4


Latin: Maior pars rerum quae in somnis innotescunt aut per purgationem animae: aut per indemoniationem: aut per meram spiritus revelationem innotescunt.
Translation:
Most of the things which become known in dreams become known either through purification of the soul, or through possession by spirits, or through pure revelation of a spirit.

Exegesis:
This reflects a tripartite epistemology of dreams, rooted in Arabic dream theory (e.g., from Avicenna or medieval Chaldean sources). Dreams can reveal truths via:
1. Purification – the soul, made clear, reflects intelligible reality.
2. Possession – the dreamer is overwhelmed or entered by a daimonic being (a positive or negative force).
3. Revelation – some truths are given directly by spiritual entities, bypassing reason.

Pico shows an openness to esoteric and even magical epistemologies—common among Chaldean, Hermetic, and Sufi thinkers—and affirms dreams as one valid mode of spiritual cognition.

I.21.5


Latin: Quod scribit Tabet Chaldeus de dormitione super Epar in revelatione somniorum recte intelligetur si ad hoc dictum dicta Platonis in Timeo concordaverimus.
Translation:
What Tabet the Chaldean wrote about sleep, concerning Epar, in the revelation of dreams, will be rightly understood if we harmonize his saying with the sayings of Plato in the Timaeus.

Exegesis:
Pico refers to a (possibly legendary or apocryphal) Chaldean sage named Tabet and a figure called Epar, probably standing in for ancient Mesopotamian or Hermetic sources. He suggests that their dream theories align with Plato’s Timaeus, which posits that the rational soul retreats during sleep, leaving the imaginative faculty dominant. Harmonization (concordia) is central to Pico’s syncretic method—bridging Chaldean/Oriental wisdom with Greek philosophy.

I.21.6


Latin: Quia sicut dixit Abdala videre somnia est fortitudo imaginationis: intelligere ea est fortitudo intellectus: ideo qui videt ea ut plurimum non intelligit ea.
Translation:
As Abdala said, to see dreams is a power of the imagination; to understand them is a power of the intellect; thus, the one who sees them usually does not understand them.

Exegesis:
"Abdala" might refer to an Arabic sage or a Latinization of ʿAbd Allāh. The point is epistemological: imaginatio and intellectus are distinct faculties. Dreamers may have vivid experiences (imaginative strength) but lack the noetic clarity to interpret them. This highlights the importance of hermeneutics of dreaming, and the idea that true understanding requires a separate operation of the rational soul or the agent intellect.

I.21.7


Latin: Anima est fons motus et gubernatrix materiae.
Translation:
The soul is the source of motion and the governor of matter.

Exegesis:
This is a classical Aristotelian and Platonic thesis, with added emphasis on governance (gubernatrix). The soul is not merely the principle of life (as in Aristotle’s De Anima), but also the cosmic principle of order—ruling over the chaos of matter. It also echoes Stoic and Hermetic thought, where the soul mediates between divine mind and the material world, instilling logos (rational form) into chaotic nature.

I.21.8


Latin: Transcorporationem animarum crediderunt omnes sapientes Indorum Persarum Aegiptiorum et Chaldeorum.
Translation:
All the sages of the Indians, Persians, Egyptians, and Chaldeans believed in the transmigration of souls.

Exegesis:
Pico acknowledges the ancient doctrine of metempsychosis—the soul’s passage through various bodies over time—as a widespread belief among the wise of many ancient cultures. By listing India, Persia, Egypt, and Chaldea, Pico constructs a lineage of prisca theologia, a primordial wisdom tradition. He affirms the idea that the soul’s journey through incarnations is essential for its purification and ascent, aligning with Neoplatonic and Orphic views.

Overall Significance:


These eight conclusions reflect Pico’s goal of synthesizing diverse intellectual traditions—Greek, Islamic, Hermetic, and Christian—into a unified account of the soul and its knowledge. Central themes include:

  • The dual nature of the soul (high and low).

  • The role of intellect versus imagination.

  • The epistemic function of dreams and visions.

  • The idea that external experience awakens internal truths.

  • The common heritage of ancient esoteric doctrines across cultures.


This is all in keeping with Pico’s broader project in the Oration on the Dignity of Man: to affirm human potential as divine, capable of grasping universal truth through the soul’s ascent and integration of all traditions.

Below is a translation and philosophical exegesis of the ten annotated conclusions from Pico della Mirandola’s 900 Theses, under the heading:

Conclusiones secundum opinionem propriam in doctrina Abucaten Avenan qui dicitur auctor de causis

(Conclusions according to my own opinion in the doctrine of Abucaten Avenan, who is called the Author of the Causes)

The name "Abucaten Avenan" likely refers to a composite figure drawn from Arabic sources, possibly blending Avicenna (Ibn Sīnā), the pseudo-Aristotle of the Liber de Causis, and elements of Plotinus or Proclus filtered through Neoplatonic Arabic philosophy. Pico is engaging these authorities critically, offering his own synthesis (opinio propria) in a way that weaves Christian Platonism with Islamic Neoplatonism.

II.6.1


Latin:
Cum dixit Abucaten Avenan animam esse supra tempus: intelligendum est de anima absoluta secundum substantiam praescindendo ab omni operatione quae competit ei inquantum est anima.

Translation:
When Abucaten Avenan said that the soul is beyond time, this must be understood of the soul considered in itself, absolutely, according to its substance, abstracting from all operations that pertain to it insofar as it is a soul.

Exegesis:
Pico distinguishes between the substantial soul and the operative soul. In its essence, the soul is eternal or supra-temporal, a view consistent with Avicennian and Neoplatonic metaphysics. But this transcendence applies only when considering the soul as a pure substance—not when examining its temporal operations like sensation or imagination. It recalls Boethius’ view of eternity as the “complete possession of life all at once,” and Avicenna’s sense of the soul as an immaterial entity existing outside temporal succession.

II.6.2


Latin:
Cum dicit Abucaten. Omnis anima nobilis tres habet operationes: divinam, intellectualem, et animalem: ita est intelligendum quod primam habeat per imaginem proportionalitatis, secundam per formalitatem participationis, tertiam per proprietatem essentialitatis.

Translation:
When Abucaten says that every noble soul has three operations—divine, intellectual, and animal—this should be understood as follows: it has the first through an image of proportionality, the second through the formality of participation, and the third through the property of essentiality.

Exegesis:
Here Pico uses a three-tiered model of the soul's activity:

  • Divine operation (divina): via imago proportionalitatis—a reflection or analogy of divine function (not direct identity).

  • Intellectual operation: by participatio, echoing Platonic metaphysics where souls “participate” in higher Forms.

  • Animal operation: through essentialitas, indicating that its embodied, sensitive life is proper to its nature.


This elaborates a Platonic ladder of ascent: from bodily activity (essential), to reasoning (participated), to spiritual vision (proportional image of divinity).

II.6.3


Latin:
Cum dixit Abucaten Omnem causam primariam plus influere: Per plus intelligas eminentiam modi causandi et intimitatem eius quod in re producitur.

Translation:
When Abucaten says that every primary cause has greater influence, by "greater" one should understand the eminence of the mode of causation and the intimacy of what is produced in the thing.

Exegesis:
Pico interprets “greater influence” not as quantity but as qualitative superiority:

  • Eminence: The higher the cause, the more noble and formally intense its causation.

  • Intimacy: The more deeply the effect is imprinted in its product, the more fundamental the causation.


This reads the Neoplatonic chain of being metaphysically: the First Cause produces all things more profoundly than any intermediate cause could, because it gives being itself.

II.6.4


Latin:
Quamvis dicat Abucaten quod esse quod est primum creatum est super intelligentiam: non credas tamen illud secundum Hypostasim esse distinctum ab intelligentia.

Translation:
Although Abucaten says that the being which is the first created is beyond intelligence, do not believe that it is, according to hypostasis, distinct from intelligence.

Exegesis:
Here Pico parses a difficult Neoplatonic problem: the first created being (ens primum creatum) is not beyond the intellect as another hypostasis (level of being), but rather is intellect itself in its highest form.

He corrects a misunderstanding: even if this "first being" is beyond human intellection, it is not metaphysically separate from intelligence—it is identical to the divine Intellect (Nous), as in Plotinus and the Liber de Causis.

II.6.5


Latin:
Cum dixit Abucaten causam primam superiorem esse omni narratione: non tam propter id habet veritatem quod primo affert quia scilicet causam ante se non habet quam propter id quod secundario innuit: quia omne intelligibile unialiter antecedit.

Translation:
When Abucaten says that the first cause is beyond all narration, this is true not so much because it has no cause before it (as he first suggests), but more truly because it precedes all intelligibles in a unified manner.

Exegesis:
Pico deepens the Neoplatonic apophatic view: the First Cause is ineffable not just because it is uncaused, but because it transcends all modes of being and knowing. It precedes all intelligibles not by exclusion, but by being the unified ground of all intelligibility—what Proclus calls the One before the One.

II.6.6


Latin:
Quod dicit Abucaten: intelligentiam esse substantiam quae non dividitur: maxime est verum per indiscretam in ea intelligibilium ad invicem penetrationem.

Translation:
What Abucaten says—that intelligence is a substance which is not divided—is especially true because of the undiscriminated interpenetration of intelligibles within it.

Exegesis:
This points to the simplicity of divine or noetic intellect. In human cognition, we know through separation—subject from object, concept from concept. But in intellectus (as divine or angelic), all forms are present simultaneously and interpenetrating, like a light in which no part is separate from another. This is a central idea in Plotinus, where the Nous contains all Forms in unity.

II.6.7


Latin:
Ex praecedenti conclusione potest haberi quomodo intelligendum est dictum Abucaten: quod omnis intelligentia est plena formis.

Translation:
From the preceding conclusion we can understand what Abucaten means when he says: every intelligence is full of forms.

Exegesis:
Building on II.6.6, Pico clarifies that to say intelligence is "full of forms" does not mean it contains many things divided, but rather that all forms are present in unity, as co-present potentialities. This anticipates the Christian idea of the divine ideas in God and the Proclean sense of intellect as a plenitude of forms (pleroma).

II.6.8


Latin:
Per ultimam propositionem Abucaten possumus intelligere quid sibi velit illa divisio: quam fecit Plato in principio tractatus Timaei: et possumus scire quod sub ea non comprehenditur anima nisi per viam extremalis conbinationis.

Translation:
Through Abucaten’s final proposition, we can understand the division made by Plato at the beginning of the Timaeus, and we can know that under it the soul is not comprehended except by way of extreme combination.

Exegesis:
Pico references Plato’s threefold division in the Timaeus: Being, Becoming, and the Receptacle. He asserts that soul is not fully accounted for by any one part—it lies at the junction between intelligible and sensible realms. This supports the mediating role of soul in Platonic cosmology, often described as a third term (between Nous and Nature).

II.6.9


Latin:
Ex antepenultima propositione Abucaten colligi potest quod declinare plus ad sensum quam ad intellectum non est animae ut anima est: sed ut cadens est.

Translation:
From the third-to-last proposition of Abucaten it can be concluded that leaning more toward sense than intellect does not belong to the soul as soul, but as fallen.

Exegesis:
This conclusion draws a sharp line between the true nature of the soul (intellectual, upward-tending) and its fallen condition (immersed in sensation and body). It reflects Christian Neoplatonic anthropology, where the soul is in exile—and its enslavement to the senses is the result of its descent (cadens), not its essence.

II.6.10


Latin:
Cum dicit Abucaten intelligentiam ut divina est regere res: intelligendum est de regimine statuitivo non ordinativo qui ei conpetit ut intelligentia est.

Translation:
When Abucaten says that intelligence, insofar as it is divine, governs things, this must be understood as a determining governance, not an ordering governance, which belongs to it as intelligence.

Exegesis:
Pico distinguishes two kinds of governance:

  • Statuitivum (determinative): divine intellect sets the essence, order, and identity of things.

  • Ordinativum (dispositional): implies temporal or providential arrangement.


The divine intellect does not micromanage the cosmos—it establishes its structure in a timeless act of intellection. This is a metaphysical rather than temporal governance, akin to the eternal Forms shaping temporal becoming.

Final Reflection:


These ten propositions reveal Pico’s philosophical refinement of Liber de Causis-style Neoplatonism through a Christian-Platonist lens. He upholds:

  • The supra-temporal nature of soul and intellect

  • The unity of intelligibles in the divine mind

  • The causal hierarchy of being rooted in metaphysical intimacy

  • The mediating and fallen condition of the human soul

  • The divine as uncaused, ineffable, and the source of all form and order


Pico’s syncretism is rigorous, speculative, and deeply metaphysical—fitting for a thinker who aimed to reconcile all schools of wisdom in a single humanistic-theological system.

Here's a translation and philosophical exegesis of the 41 annotated theses under the heading:

Conclusiones secundum Averroem numero .xli.

(Conclusions according to Averroes, number 41)

These conclusions reflect Pico’s engagement with Averroes (Ibn Rushd), whose rigorous Aristotelianism and commentaries deeply shaped medieval Latin philosophy. However, Pico critiques and revises many Averroist positions from his own syncretic Christian-Neoplatonic perspective. I’ll provide translation and analysis grouped for clarity, focusing especially on key metaphysical, epistemological, and cosmological themes.

PROPHETIC & INTELLECTUAL SOUL

I.7.1


Latin: Possibilis est prophetia in somnis per illustrationem intellectus agentis super animam nostram.
Translation:
Prophecy in dreams is possible through the illumination of our soul by the agent intellect.

Exegesis:
Averroes (influenced by Avicenna) viewed dreams as potential vehicles of prophecy when the agent intellect impresses forms onto the passive imagination. The intellectus agens acts as a divine transmitter of truth. This view stands close to Neoplatonism and supports the prophetic function of the philosopher-sage.

I.7.2


Latin: Una est anima intellectiva in omnibus hominibus.
Translation:
There is one intellective soul in all human beings.

Exegesis:
This is Averroes’s famous doctrine of the unity of the intellect—there is only one eternal, shared intellectus possibilis (potential intellect) for all people. Individual thought is the result of this intellect acting through particular faculties. This was controversial, especially for Christian theologians who insisted on individual immortality.

I.7.3


Latin: Felicitas ultima hominis est cum continuatur intellectus agens possibili: ut forma…
Translation:
The ultimate happiness of man is when the agent intellect is united with the possible intellect as form—yet this union was misunderstood perversely and erroneously by other Latins, especially Johannes de Jandun, who corrupted and distorted Averroes’s teaching not only here but in nearly all philosophical matters.

Exegesis:
Pico asserts the true beatitude (felicity) lies in the union of human potential intellect with the divine agent intellect—this is salvation, understood philosophically. He blames Johannes de Jandun, a major Latin Averroist, for misrepresenting this idea. Pico, unlike strict Averroists, tries to reconcile intellectual beatitude with personal immortality and Christian faith.

I.7.4


Latin: Possibile est tenendo unitatem intellectus: animam meam ita particulariter meam… remanere post mortem.
Translation:
It is possible—while affirming the unity of the intellect—for my soul, in such a way particularly mine and not common to all, to remain after death.

Exegesis:
Pico insists personal survival is possible even if intellect is in some sense universal. He suggests a synthesis: intellect may be shared, but the soul retains personal identity, preserving immortality. This reflects Christian concerns with individuality and resurrection.

METAPHYSICS & CAUSALITY

I.7.5


Translation:
Every abstract being depends on the first abstract in three kinds of causes: formal, final, and efficient.

Exegesis:
Pico attributes to Averroes a fully Neoplatonic causal hierarchy—every abstract entity derives its form, purpose, and act of coming-to-be from the First Cause, likely referring to the Intellect or God. It echoes Proclus and the Liber de Causis.

I.7.6


Translation:
It is impossible for the same species to be generated both through propagation and putrefaction.

Exegesis:
Averroes held that species have determinate modes of generation. Pico agrees: a rational animal cannot arise both through normal procreation and decay (spontaneous generation). This defends a more teleological biology.

I.7.7


Translation:
God moves the first heaven not only as final cause, but also as true efficient and proper mover.

Exegesis:
Against Aristotle’s “unmoved mover” as merely a final cause, Pico (citing Averroes) affirms God is an efficient cause—a real mover of the cosmos. This leans toward a more personalistic theism.

CELESTIAL PSYCHOLOGY & COSMOLOGY

I.7.8


Translation:
Every mover of the heavens is the soul of its own sphere, making with it a greater substantial unity than that of the soul of an ox with its body.

As Saif shows, early modern philosophers were not slavish adopters of Arabic thought—they translated, syncretized, and rethematized it within their own spiritual and metaphysical goals.

These ideas were essential to magical epistemology, where knowing the sympathies of heaven and soul enabled action at a distance.

This echoes Avicennian psychology but introduces a moral and soteriological dimension: the fallen part of the soul is subject to astral influence, but the higher intellect is free and can ascend. This move integrates Arabic causality into a Christian teleology of ascent and moral purification, a theme Saif identifies in both Ficino and Pico.

For them, the intellectus agens functions as a divine transmitter of intelligible forms into the imaginative faculty. Pico’s retention of this thesis suggests that he accepts the possibility of natural prophecy—visions not from demons or divine miracles, but from the celestial mind. Instead, Pico’s Conclusiones show a deep philosophical engagement with Arabic sources as authorities on astral causality, soul metaphysics, and magical theory. In doing so, Pico stands as a model of the transcultural transmission of esoteric knowledge, one who recognized Arabic philosophy as a legitimate and fertile source for Christian magic and theology—not in opposition to faith, but as part of the divine unfolding of wisdom across the ages.

✦ Commentary on Pico’s Conclusiones secundum Avicennam numero XII

These conclusions reflect Pico's nuanced reading of Avicenna (Ibn Sīnā), the towering figure of Islamic peripatetic philosophy. While often received in Latin Scholasticism as a source of metaphysics and psychology, Avicenna was also deeply influential on the philosophy of logic, cosmology, and natural science, all of which appear in Pico’s distillation. Pico does not merely repeat Avicenna’s ideas—he engages them critically and often positions them in relation to broader philosophical or theological concerns, as Liana Saif’s analysis encourages us to see.

I.8.1–2. Avicennan Innovations in Logic

I.8.1


Praeter syllogismum categoricum et hypotheticum: datur genus syllogismorum compositivorum.
Translation:
Beyond categorical and hypothetical syllogisms, there exists a third kind: compositive syllogisms.

I.8.2


Licet in nullo syllogismo qui sit actu vel potentia categoricus ex duabus negativis concludi possit: potest tamen hoc fieri in syllogismo compositivo: ut scilicet ex duabus negativis concludatur.
Translation:
Although in no actual or potential categorical syllogism can a conclusion be drawn from two negatives, this can occur in a compositive syllogism: namely, a conclusion from two negative premises.

Commentary:
These conclusions address Avicenna’s contributions to modal and composite logic, where he expanded the Aristotelian framework with new syllogistic structures. The idea of a "compositive syllogism" (qiyās tarkībī) is Avicenna’s innovation, allowing inference from combinations typically ruled out in categorical logic. Pico takes this as an opportunity to deepen the logical tools available to the metaphysician, magician, or theologian—broadening the field of valid reasoning in the face of occult or liminal phenomena that defy Aristotelian norms.

As Saif argues, this flexibility in logic opened the door to rationalizing non-standard modes of knowledge, such as prophecy or astrology, which require analogical and symbolic reasoning beyond syllogistic orthodoxy.

I.8.3–6. Natural Philosophy and Ontology

I.8.3


In coelo est materia eiusdem rationis cum materia inferiorum.
Translation:
There is in the heavens matter of the same kind as that of lower things.

Commentary:
This controversial claim deviates from Aristotelian orthodoxy, which held that celestial bodies are composed of a unique, incorruptible fifth element (aether). Avicenna challenged this in places, allowing for a kind of universal hylomorphism (all things are composed of matter and form), which Pico takes up—perhaps to argue that the heavens are not wholly separate, and thus accessible to human knowledge and even magical influence. This links to Saif’s point that Avicennian cosmology blurred lines between celestial and terrestrial causality, legitimizing occult correspondences.

I.8.4


Non potest in anima esse notio intelligibilis sine actuali intellectione.
Translation:
There can be no intelligible notion in the soul without an actual act of understanding.

Commentary:
This aligns with Avicenna’s view that intelligibles exist in act only when the intellect is engaged—rejecting Platonic innatism. For Pico, it supports the idea that knowledge is a dynamic actualization, not a static possession. This has implications for magic and mystical ascent: the soul must undergo operations to rise to true knowledge.

I.8.5


Possibile est hominem ex putrefactione generari.
Translation:
It is possible for a human to be generated from putrefaction.

Commentary:
This reflects Avicennian biology and the theory of spontaneous generation, inherited from Aristotle. It reveals that form can emerge from corrupted matter under certain conditions—a notion that fascinated Renaissance thinkers who saw transformation, alchemical or magical, as natural rather than miraculous.

I.8.6


Essentia rei: materiam propriam et formam complectitur.
Translation:
The essence of a thing comprises its proper matter and form.

Commentary:
This is a classic Avicennian definition of composite essence. Unlike Platonic essences, Avicenna insists that a full definition requires both matter and form, grounding essence in the world of generation and corruption. Pico adopts this to undergird metaphysical realism in a way that still allows for spiritual ascent and individuation.

I.8.7


Prima substantia prior est qualibet substantia habente habitudinem ad operationem transeuntem…
Translation:
The first substance is prior to any substance having a relation to a transitive operation, of whatever kind—formal, material, efficient, or final.

I.8.8


Ab uno simplici in fine simplicitatis non provenit nisi unum.
Translation:
From one that is simple in the highest degree, only one can proceed.

Commentary:
These are pure Avicennian metaphysics. The first (I.8.7) affirms that God, as the First Substance, precedes all beings involved in causal operations. The second (I.8.8) is the foundational Avicennian principle of emanation: the first cause (absolutely simple) can produce only one effect directly—namely, the first intelligence—because multiplicity would imply complexity in the cause.

As Saif emphasizes, this emanationism undergirded astrological and magical models in Arabic and Latin thought. The chain of being from One to many structures the occult worldview Pico inherits.

Translation:

Organum odoratus sunt carunculae mamillares in anteriori cerebri parte constitutae.
Translation:
The organ of smell consists of the mamillary caruncles located at the front part of the brain.

Commentary:
These reflect Avicenna’s careful physiological work in the Canon of Medicine and De Anima. The distinction between real being and intentional being of odor reflects Avicennian epistemology: a thing may exist as a physical object and also as a perceived form. The location of the organ of smell in the mamillary bodies shows a remarkable physiological insight—remarkably prescient for the time.

Pico’s inclusion of these theses signals his interest in a scientific foundation for spiritual and magical theories of sensation. Saif discusses how early modern occult philosophers grounded spiritual faculties in natural, often Avicennian, models of perception and imagination.

Commentary:
Avicenna often criticized Aristotle’s treatment of modal logic, offering more nuanced views on the conversion of propositions. Pico cites this to show that modal reasoning—especially in areas of metaphysics, prophecy, and divine knowledge—requires greater subtlety than Aristotelian logic allows. As Saif notes, this flexibility in modal logic made room for knowledge of the future, necessary for astrology, theurgy, and esoteric hermeneutics.

✦ Commentary on Pico’s Conclusiones secundum Alfarabium numero XI

Abū Naṣr al-Fārābī (c. 872–950), known as the Second Teacher (after Aristotle), was a key transmitter and systematizer of Aristotelian and Neoplatonic philosophy in the Islamic world. His works shaped the curriculum of logic, metaphysics, and political philosophy that would deeply influence Avicenna, Averroes, and ultimately the Latin scholastic and humanist traditions, including Pico.

Pico’s Conclusiones attributed to al-Fārābī highlight a rationalist metaphysics and epistemology grounded in logic, the structure of scientific demonstration, and a theory of intellectual perfection. Liana Saif's research helps situate this as part of the broader Arabic project of harmonizing Aristotelian logic and Neoplatonic metaphysics—a project that Pico, like Ficino, integrates into a Christianized esoteric synthesis.

LOGIC AND DEMONSTRATION (I.9.1–2)

I.9.1


Primum quod est necessarium in demonstratione...
Translation:
The first (principle) that is necessary in demonstration is not what Aristotle defined in Posterior Analytics I, but should be defined as: the first is that which is more universal than the subject, though it is not predicated as part of the subject’s genus.

Commentary:
This challenges Aristotle’s own definition of the principle of demonstration, suggesting a more abstract universal that does not function as a genus. Al-Fārābī often refined Aristotle’s logic, emphasizing the need for super-genus universals in scientific knowledge. Pico, by preserving this distinction, aligns with the Arabic view that demonstration requires metaphysical clarity and not just categorical classification. This undergirds occult philosophy by legitimizing forms of non-Aristotelian deduction, as Saif discusses, particularly in astrology and metaphysical speculation.

I.9.2


Diffinitio generis quam dabit Porphyrius mala est...
Translation:
The definition of genus given by Porphyry is faulty; instead, genus should be defined as that which is more universal than two universals.

Commentary:
This echoes al-Fārābī's critique of Porphyrian logic, aiming to refine the concept of genus for more precise classification. The point is subtle but important: Pico uses this to show that Arabic logicians were not passive transmitters, but active innovators of logic, making the tools of scientific classification more robust—tools essential for organizing esoteric knowledge as scientia.

EPISTEMOLOGY AND THE PURPOSE OF KNOWLEDGE (I.9.3–7)

I.9.3


Summum hominis bonum est perfectio per scientias speculativas.
Translation:
The highest good of man is perfection through speculative sciences.

Commentary:
This is quintessential al-Fārābī, who saw human flourishing (saʿāda) as the actualization of the intellect through speculative knowledge. Pico likely agrees, but he sees this intellectual perfection as preparatory for divine union or prophetic inspiration. As Saif shows, this Arabic model of knowledge as transformative perfection shaped Renaissance occultism’s view of magic as a path to the divine through science.

I.9.4


Cum dicit Aristoteles... intelligende sunt per doctrinam et disciplinam cognitio diffinitiva et argumentativa.
Translation:
When Aristotle says that every doctrine proceeds from pre-existing knowledge, this must be understood as referring to definitive and argumentative knowledge.

Commentary:
This interprets Aristotle’s tabula rasa theory through a constructivist epistemology: science builds on defined and reasoned knowledge, not just sensation. Al-Fārābī emphasized the architecture of disciplines, and here Pico follows him, distinguishing types of intellectual progress foundational to both philosophy and magic.

I.9.5–7


These three theses focus on modal logic, definition, and necessity:
  • I.9.5 defines the intent of “saying of all” as applying to actual or potential subjects, but not necessarily.

  • I.9.6 denies the possibility of defending certain Aristotelian syllogisms unless this understanding is preserved.

  • I.9.7 equates the "possible" in Aristotle’s Prior Analytics with both possibility and invention, distinguishing it from necessity.


Commentary:
These positions show Pico’s intense engagement with modal logic, emphasizing a semantic and ontological precision that supports the rigorous metaphysics behind astrology, talismans, and prophecy. According to Saif, thinkers like al-Fārābī provided the logical scaffolding that allowed early modern magicians to defend their art as science—especially in the face of theological critique.

I.9.8


Non potest intelligi accidens etiam in abstracto non intelligendo subiectum.
Translation:
It is not possible to understand an accident, even in abstraction, without understanding the subject.

Commentary:
This reflects the ontological dependence of accidents on substances. Al-Fārābī was instrumental in clarifying how accidents are conceptually grasped, which is essential for any metaphysical system that includes imaginal forms or symbolic properties—such as those in magical correspondences.

I.9.9


Species sunt in medio: medio modo inter esse spirituale et materiale.
Translation:
Species exist in a middle way: midway between spiritual and material being.

I.9.10


Quelibet species secundum esse spirituale est formaliter cognitio.
Translation:
Each species, in its spiritual being, is formally a cognition.

✦ Commentary on Conclusiones secundum Moysem Aegyptium numero III

“Moses the Egyptian” (Moyses Aegyptius) is a likely reference to Hermes Trismegistus—identified in the Renaissance with Moses through a syncretic tradition stretching from Late Antiquity to Ficino and Pico himself. As Charles Stang and Brian Copenhaver have shown, this identification aligned Hermetic philosophy with Mosaic wisdom, allowing Renaissance thinkers to claim that ancient Egyptian theosophy was compatible with Christian truth. In this light, Pico’s three theses “according to Moses the Egyptian” can be read as a critique of Aristotelian physics and metaphysics from a Hermetic-Platonic perspective, grounded in final causality and intuitive simplicity rather than mechanical explanation.

Liana Saif’s work allows us to understand how this mode of critique echoes Arabic Neoplatonic thought, which also challenged or transcended Aristotle’s views by introducing emanative, final-causal, and spiritual frameworks of knowledge and causation.

I.12.1


Latin: Demonstratio octavi physicorum ab Aristotele ad probandum primum motorem adducta: aliquid probat speciale primo.
Translation:
The demonstration from Physics VIII, brought forward by Aristotle to prove the first mover, proves something particular first [rather than the universal].

Commentary:
Pico, under the name of Moses the Egyptian, critiques Aristotle’s cosmological argument for the Unmoved Mover, suggesting that it only demonstrates a particular mover—perhaps the mover of the outermost sphere—rather than the universal first cause. This is in line with Neoplatonic and Hermetic metaphysics, which criticize Aristotelian logic for its inability to ascend to the truly transcendent.

This is also consonant with Avicennian and Liber de Causis metaphysics, where the First is beyond motion and cannot be demonstrated through physical movement. As Saif shows, early modern occult thinkers used Arabic sources to challenge the limits of Aristotelian physics, favoring a causal hierarchy that began in divine simplicity, not motion.

I.12.2


Latin: Scientia Methaphysicae non est una scientia.
Translation:
Metaphysical science is not a single science.

Commentary:
This is a profound statement. Pico is invoking the Platonic view that being is not univocal, and thus metaphysics must be multiple—one for being as being, another for the divine, another for causes, and so forth. Moses the Egyptian, as a Hermetic-Platonic voice, would argue that the knowledge of God, of souls, of forms, and of the One are distinct yet interrelated pursuits.

This thesis contrasts sharply with Aristotle’s conception of metaphysics as a single science of being qua being. Saif’s book traces how Arabic Neoplatonists (especially in the Liber de Causis) split metaphysics across multiple ontological levels—each requiring its own mode of approach (intellective, intuitive, inspired)—a framework Pico draws on here.

I.12.3


Latin: Simplicitas primi et omnimoda immaterialitas probari non potest per causalitatem efficientem motus: sed per finalem tantum.
Translation:
The simplicity of the First and its complete immateriality cannot be proven through the efficient causality of motion, but only through final causality.

Commentary:
This returns to a classic Neoplatonic critique of Aristotle: the First cannot be understood as the cause that pushes (efficient), but as the cause that draws (final). Pico—speaking as Moses the Egyptian—claims that the divine is the final cause of all desire, motion, and being. This is Hermetic-Platonic theology: the One is not an actor, but the object of love and return.

This parallels Proclus, Plotinus, and their Arabic interpreters (e.g. Avicenna, al-Farabi, the Liber de Causis). As Saif notes, Arabic philosophers were instrumental in shifting causality away from mechanics toward metaphysical attraction, a move that profoundly influenced Renaissance magical cosmology.

✦ Hermeticism and Arabic Emanationism

Liana Saif identifies the deep entanglement of Arabic Neoplatonic metaphysics and Hermetic cosmology in the Renaissance. Pico’s “Moses the Egyptian” is not merely a cipher for ancient Egypt; it is a philosophical persona for challenging Aristotle’s empirical limitations and proposing a metaphysical system rooted in:

  • Divine unity and final causality

  • Multiple levels of being and knowledge

  • A participatory cosmos, structured hierarchically


In this light, Moses the Egyptian and the Liber de Causis both serve as spokespersons for a cosmology of emanation, not mechanical causation. This metaphysical schema undergirds Renaissance magical operations—as it establishes a universe bound together by desire, similarity, and return, not just motion and force.

✦ Conclusion: Moses the Egyptian as a Voice of Arabic-Hermetic Wisdom

These three short theses represent a philosophical critique of Aristotelian physics and metaphysics from a Hermetic-Neoplatonic standpoint—deeply compatible with Arabic metaphysics as received through the Liber de Causis, Avicenna, and others. As Saif’s work makes clear, Pico here is not rejecting Arabic influence but rather channeling it through an older symbolic figure of wisdom, Moses the Egyptian, to assert the primacy of final causality, metaphysical simplicity, and the multiplicity of modes of knowing the divine.

✦ Commentary on Conclusiones secundum priscam doctrinam Mercurii Trismegisti Aegyptii (I.27.1–10)

I.27.1


Ubicunque vita: ibi anima: ubicunque anima: ibi mens.
Translation:
Wherever there is life, there is soul; wherever there is soul, there is mind.

Commentary:
This maxim restates the Hermetic continuum of being: life → soul → mind → God. It asserts a panpsychic ontology, where all life is inherently ensouled and therefore intelligent. Saif traces how such ensouled cosmologies, common in Hermeticism, were made philosophically rigorous through Arabic Neoplatonism, particularly in the doctrine that the world is animated through intermediary intelligences—minds ordering souls, which in turn animate matter.

I.27.2


Omne motum corporeum: omne movens incorporeum.
Translation:
All that is bodily moved is moved by something incorporeal.

Commentary:
This aligns with the Neoplatonic principle of spiritual causality, but also reflects the Arabic cosmological view, especially in the Liber de Causis, that motion originates in immaterial intelligence. This undercuts purely physical explanations of nature, grounding magic in a hierarchy of incorporeal forces—essential to the justifications for astrology and talismanic influence in both Arabic and Renaissance occult theory.

I.27.3


Anima in corpore: mens in anima: in mente verbum: tum horum pater deus.
Translation:
The soul is in the body, mind is in the soul, the Word is in the mind—and God is the father of them all.

Commentary:
This encapsulates a Hermetic chain of being: body → soul → mind → Logos → God. It mirrors both Christian Trinitarian theology and Plotinian/Avicennian metaphysics, where the divine emanates through nous (intellect) and psyche (soul) into the cosmos. Saif shows how Arabic authors built such metaphysical ladders to justify occult ascent—the soul rising through mental purification to rejoin its divine origin.

I.27.4


Deus circa omnia atque per omnia: mens circa animam: anima circa aerem: aer circa materiam.
Translation:
God surrounds and penetrates all things; mind is around the soul, the soul around air, air around matter.

Commentary:
This is a Hermetic cosmograph—describing a spherical hierarchy of being, where God envelops all. The structure reflects the emanative layering of Arabic Neoplatonism, where each level transmits form and motion to the one below. Saif notes that such aetheric and pneumatic hierarchies allowed magicians to explain how divine influence enters the world through subtle intermediaries like air, stars, and imaginal forms.

I.27.5


Nihil est in mundo expers vitae.
Translation:
Nothing in the world is devoid of life.

Commentary:
A declaration of universal animation, this thesis aligns Hermetic cosmology with Arabic doctrines that even the stars and spheres are alive—souls with intellects (as in Avicenna and the Liber de Causis). As Saif stresses, this was not poetic metaphor but a scientific ontology: the occult world is alive, and magic works by recognizing and working with these vital forces.

I.27.6


Nihil est in universo passibile mortis vel corruptionis.
Corollary: Ubique vita: ubique providentia: ubique immortalitas.
Translation:
Nothing in the universe is subject to death or corruption.
Corollary: Everywhere is life, everywhere providence, everywhere immortality.

Commentary:
This echoes the eternalism of Hermetic and Arabic cosmology. In the sublunar world, corruption seems real—but in truth, the deeper reality is immortality and providential order. Pico affirms the unbroken chain of divine care, found equally in Hermetic thought and Arabic metaphysics. Saif explores how this worldview made astrology and prophecy intelligible: the universe is ordered, alive, and communicative.

I.27.7


Sex viis futura homini deus denuntiat: per Somnia: Portenta: Aves: Intestina: spiritum et Sybillam.
Translation:
God announces the future to man in six ways: through dreams, omens, birds, entrails, spirit, and sibyl.

Commentary:
This is a taxonomy of divinatory methods, directly linked to Hermetic and Arabic prophetic theory. The combination of natural (birds, entrails) and spiritual (dreams, spirit, sibyl) mirrors Avicennian ideas of prophecy as both natural and supernatural. Saif notes how Renaissance occultists like Ficino and Pico used these systems to defend pagan divination as part of the universal language of Providence.

I.27.8


Verum est... intransmutabile bonum ac penitus incorporeum.
Translation:
Truth is that which is unperturbed, undetermined, uncolored, unshaped, unshaken, naked, transparent, self-comprehensible, unchangeable good, and utterly incorporeal.

Commentary:
This definition of Truth mirrors the Plotinian One, the Avicennian Necessary Existent, and the Hermetic God. It stands outside multiplicity and perception. Pico thus invokes a negative theology—Truth as the utterly transcendent source. As Saif shows, this theology justifies magic not as manipulation, but as a return to the incorporeal through purification.

I.27.9


Decem intra unumquemque sunt ultores...
Translation:
Ten avengers dwell within each person: ignorance, sadness, inconstancy, greed, injustice, lust, deception, envy, fraud, anger, rashness, wickedness.

Commentary:
These are inner daemons—vices or passions—reminiscent of both Stoic ethics and Hermetic spiritual psychology. The soul’s purification requires overcoming these internal enemies. This inner moral struggle was seen by Arabic philosophers as necessary for intellectual ascent. Saif documents how this ascetic psychology undergirded the path of magical initiation and wisdom.

I.27.10


Decem ultores... correspondere male coordinationi denariae in cabala...
Translation:
The ten avengers of the previous thesis, according to Mercury, will be seen by the deep contemplator to correspond to the corrupt tenfold ordering in the Kabbalah—and to its rulers, about which I say nothing in the cabalistic conclusions, because it is secret.

Commentary:
This is a moment of syncretic brilliance: Pico links Hermetic evil forces with the Qliphothic side of the Kabbalistic Tree of Life—the realm of impure powers that distort divine emanation. By withholding the full teaching, Pico alludes to the esoteric nature of Kabbalah, yet he reveals a Hermetic-Kabbalistic cosmology of spiritual combat.

Liana Saif’s The Arabic Influences on Early Modern Occult Philosophy offers a rich account of how Giovanni Pico della Mirandola’s thought was deeply informed by Arabic sources—especially within the context of astral magic, metaphysical speculation, and the transmission of the Hermetic tradition. Here are key aspects of Saif's interpretation that illuminate Pico’s engagement with Arabic philosophy and magic:

1. Pico’s Syncretic Project and Arabic Sources

Saif underscores that Pico's syncretism—his effort to unify and reconcile different philosophical and religious systems—was not limited to classical antiquity or Christian scholasticism. Rather, it actively embraced Arabic authors such as:

  • Abu Maʿshar (al-Falaki), especially Kitāb al-Madkhal al-Kabīr (Great Introduction to the Science of Astrology), which theorized astral causality and the ontological influence of celestial bodies.

  • Al-Kindi (De radiis), a central text in the theory of natural magic through rays and spiritual emanation.

  • Thabit ibn Qurra and the Picatrix, which bound together magical recipes with philosophical ideas.

  • Avicenna and Averroes, whose metaphysics and psychology shaped debates over the unity of the intellect and the status of prophecy, dreams, and imagination.


These Arabic thinkers helped Pico frame occult concepts like the intellectus agens, the role of the celestial intelligences, and the epistemological structure of magic and prophecy【25:3†source】【25:10†source】.

2. Arabic Hermeticism vs. Latin Hermeticism

Saif makes a key distinction between Arabic Hermeticism and the Latin reception of the Corpus Hermeticum. She shows that Arabic texts often identified Hermes Trismegistus with the prophet Idris, and that Arabic Hermeticism was primarily practical—centered on talismans, decans, and astral influences—whereas the Latin Corpus Hermeticum was philosophical and theological in tone.

Pico was aware of both strands: while he valorized the prisca theologia of Hermes, his magical conclusions were informed just as much by Arabic theories of astral generation, dream prophecy, and the metaphysical interplay between intellectus, anima, and forma. This made his “Hermeticism” distinct from Ficino’s more Platonic-Hermetic focus【25:6†source】【25:9†source】.

3. Philosophical Uses of Astral Causality

Pico appropriated Arabic theories of causation, especially those articulated by:

  • Avicenna: On the structure of necessity, emanation, and the intermediary nature of intelligences.

  • Averroes: On the unity of the intellect and its relation to the agent intellect, and on the interpretation of dreams as spiritual revelations.

  • Al-Farabi: On the hierarchy of knowledge, modes of demonstration, and the perfection of man through speculative science.


Saif shows how these concepts were not merely academic for Pico—they undergirded his vision of magic as a philosophical science, continuous with metaphysics and theology【25:3†source】【25:13†source】.

4. Magic, Prophecy, and the Epistemology of the Soul

A major contribution of Arabic sources to Pico’s Conclusions, according to Saif, is the epistemological understanding of magic through the soul’s powers:

  • Magic becomes possible not through demonic pacts but via the imaginatio, intellectus, and phantasia in correspondence with celestial powers.

  • This is grounded in theories from thinkers like al-Kindi, Avicenna, and al-Qurtubi, who explain how celestial forms imprint upon the soul, allowing for prophecy, dream interpretation, and magical efficacy.

  • Pico’s discussions of dreams, spirits, and somnial revelations (e.g. annotated 21.4–21.6) echo Arabic classifications of dream types and visionary cognition【25:10†source】.


5. Critique of Ficino and Arabic Alternatives

Pico was critical of Ficino’s astral determinism, which he found too reliant on materialistic interpretations of celestial influence. Instead, Pico drew on Arabic authors to stress intellectual freedom and divine illumination, asserting the soul’s power to transcend astral necessity:

“Ficino’s application of astral causation to particular events and individuals” was too deterministic, whereas Pico, informed by Arabic epistemologies, affirmed that astral influence was real but not omnipotent【25:3†source】.

6. Continuity with Latin Scholasticism and the Arab-Latin Tradition

Saif shows that Pico stands at the culmination of a long Arab-Latin scholastic tradition in which astrology, philosophy, and theology were not antagonistic, but mutually reinforcing. He inherited the legacy of Albertus Magnus, Thomas Aquinas, and Roger Bacon—each of whom incorporated Arabic astrological and magical thought into Christian frameworks【25:13†source】.

Final Thought

Saif’s reading of Pico positions him not just as a Renaissance magus, but as a translator of cultures—someone who internalized and reconfigured Arabic philosophy into a universalizing Christian esoteric system. His Conclusiones represent a map of the world’s metaphysical heritage, in which Arabic philosophers are crucial guides, not marginal footnotes. Far from being derivative, Pico’s Arabic citations were the scaffolding of a new magical humanism that sought to redeem knowledge itself.
